Why This Exists

The QL + MVG pilot has a well-developed operational backbone. The Quantum Leap team has built a detailed execution playbook — 18 engagement moments, persona-tagged activities, RACI, tools, and a full operating model glossary. The Delivery Governance Team (Stephanie Flasch) is building governance tracking, a weekly cohort digest, and a Slack-based Trail Guide.

What the pilot doesn't have is a connected onboarding and skills layer — the enablement that answers whether consultants are capable of executing that playbook well under agentic conditions. Stephanie flagged it directly: consultants need enablement on the MVG model itself, not just on delivery. Kristen named the gap explicitly: no one is working on that middle lane.

The "Yes And" Structure

This pilot enablement is Track 1 — minimum viable, June 1 ready. The full three-track program (Base Layer, Proficiency Track, Immersive Cohort) is Track 2, built for scale after the pilot proves the model. Nothing here is disposable — the three deliverables and data collection mechanisms all become direct inputs to the full program design.

MVG Orientation Brief

For consultants onboarding to a QL + MVG pilot project.

The Minimum Viable Governance framework is not a compliance layer. It is the structure that makes AI-native delivery repeatable rather than lucky. MVG gives the engagement its spine — a set of phase gates where humans make deliberate decisions before work advances. Without it, agentic delivery is fast but ungoverned. With it, speed and rigor coexist.

The Governing Logic

Agents execute quickly, plausibly, and without self-doubt — the gates are where humans catch what agents cannot.

What Changes in AI-Native Delivery

  • Build compresses — agents do construction work that previously took weeks
  • Intent Definition becomes a first-class phase — what you ask agents to do matters more than how fast they do it
  • Refinement and Validation become the new critical path — a continuous loop, not a phase-end check
  • The gates become more consequential — errors amplified by agent velocity are expensive to catch late

What Stays the Same

  • The customer still needs to own the outcome
  • The consultant is still accountable for quality and fit
  • The engagement still fails if the wrong thing is built fast
Human-led, agent-powered

Agents accelerate execution. Consultants lead strategy, validate output, and govern the decisions that cannot be automated.

MVG as the Organizing Spine

Every skill in this framework exists because MVG requires it. Here is that connection made explicit.

The Minimum Viable Governance framework is not a backdrop for skills enablement — it is the organizing logic. Each MVG gate creates a distinct set of skill demands. What a consultant needs to do well in Phase 0 is different from what Phase 3 requires. And the more agentic the delivery, the more judgment-intensive each checkpoint becomes.

Why Skill Demand Intensifies Under Agentic Conditions

In traditional delivery, human review cycles absorb many risks informally — a developer asks a clarifying question, a PM notices scope creep, a QA reviewer catches errors. Agentic delivery removes that absorption layer. Agents don't ask clarifying questions. They don't flag scope expansion. They don't know the design decisions behind their own output. The skills below are the human layer that replaces what was absorbed informally.

The Skill Demand Across the Lifecycle

The Common Failure Pattern

Across every skill, the failure mode follows the same logic: speed creates pressure to skip the human-intensive step the skill is designed to protect.

Intent Writing
gets skipped because prompting feels faster
Output Validation
gets skipped because the output looks good
Change Co-Creation
gets skipped because the AI already produced something
Customer AI Readiness
gets skipped because the customer asked for AI-native delivery
Data Quality Judgment
gets assumed rather than assessed
Discernment
gets bypassed because polished output signals correctness

These are not knowledge gaps — they are habits under pressure. Knowing what discernment is does not make a consultant apply it at Phase 3 velocity when a customer is in the room and the release plan is due. The gate cards below are designed to interrupt that pattern at each stop.

Gate-by-Gate Flight Guide

Each gate card covers the full stop: what to land with, the skills that are load-bearing, the gate decision, and what to carry into the next phase.

Pre-Flight Before the Engagement Begins
The One Question That Determines Everything

Is this customer actually ready for AI-native delivery? Before Phase 0 opens, the consultant must assess whether the engagement should proceed under an AI-native model at all. The Quantum Leap program defines five readiness conditions: asset and data readiness, governance orientation, rapid decisioning capability, willingness to experiment, and contracts in order.

QL Playbook — What's Happening Here

Pre-sales is DA-led. The DA runs Scopezilla to generate the Scope Context package — discovery material, gap list, and assumptions — that the pod inherits at M01. The Customer Readiness Checklist (Victoria Gailey) is the pilot's formal readiness artifact at this stage. The skill demand is in the DA's assessment judgment: is the scope coherent enough to hand off, and is the customer situation ready for AI-native delivery?

Skill in Focus
Customer AI Readiness Assessment
Phase 0 Internal Onboarding Gate: Validated Success Criteria
Landing — What to Arrive With

Before this gate closes, the team needs a shared, measurable definition of success in outcome terms, not deliverable terms. The success criteria validated here are the quality standard every downstream gate will be measured against. If they're vague now, every gate that follows has no teeth.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
Outcome FramingSuccess criteria must describe what changes for the customer — not what gets built.
Intent WritingThe Phase 1 roadmap is grounded in intent established here. Vague intent at Phase 0 produces unpredictable agent behavior downstream.
Data Quality JudgmentBefore agents are scoped for any task, the data must be assessed as fit for agentic use — not just available.
Gate Question

Does the team have a shared, measurable definition of success — and has the customer's data been assessed as fit for the agentic workflows being planned?

QL Playbook — What's Happening Here

M01 Handoff: DA transfers the Scopezilla package to the IA in a 60-minute session. The IA's first judgment call is whether the package is coherent enough to proceed — this is discernment in its earliest form. M02 Mobilization: the full pod absorbs the pre-sales material together (≤3 working days), augments the gap list, and stands up all tooling (Claude Code, MCP connectors, NotebookLM, MeshMesh, GitHub repo, build sandbox). The Engagement Roadmap is drafted here, ready for customer sign-off. M03 Pod-Ready Gate: PL signs off that the pod is ready before the customer enters Phase 1. Phase 1 does not open until every M03 item is checked.

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 1

The success criteria validated here are the anchor for every epic in the Phase 1 roadmap. Take them into the first customer session and make them visible. If a proposed epic can't be traced to one of these criteria, that's a scope question — not a delivery task.

Phase 1 Prepare Gate: Customer-Approved Engagement Roadmap (Epics)
Landing — What to Arrive With

The roadmap the customer approves here must be one they helped build — not one they were asked to sign off on. A roadmap the customer co-authored creates ownership. A roadmap the customer approved creates a future scope dispute.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
Change Co-CreationThe roadmap must be co-authored in working sessions, not presented for approval.
Outcome FramingEach epic should trace to an outcome, not a feature. The roadmap is a value realization plan, not a task list.
Intent WritingCustomer-approved epics become the primary input to agent-assisted design in Phase 2. Imprecise epics produce unpredictable agent output.
Systems ThinkingThe roadmap should reflect how the customer's systems, data, and people interact — not just what the software will do.
Gate Question

Would a team member joining mid-stream — or an agent acting on this roadmap — understand exactly what success looks like for each epic, and is the scope bounded enough to deliver it?

QL Playbook — What's Happening Here

M04 Customer Kickoff: the first time the customer enters a working session with the pod. The IA and PL walk the Engagement Roadmap (drafted in M02); the Executive Sponsor signs it; the Customer Readiness Checklist is signed. This is a stage gate — Phase 2 formally opens here. M05 Write Intent (Gap-Closing): the pod runs gap-closing sessions with the customer, working from the augmented gap list built in M02. Every gap exits as answered (becomes an Intent Statement), deferred (risk-registered), or escalated (M10). The IA drafts each Intent Statement with the draft-agent; the customer confirms in writing. M06 Build-Ready Gate: PL signs that ≥3 days of customer-confirmed Intent Statements exist before Build Day 1 is scheduled.

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 2

The epics approved here become the design brief for Phase 2. Before agents begin generating architecture artifacts, each epic needs a well-formed intent statement — not just an epic label. The first task of Phase 2 is translating customer-approved epics into agent-ready intent.

Phase 2 Design Gate: Architecture Sign-Off
Landing — What to Arrive With

Agents begin doing real construction in this phase — generating solution designs, architecture artifacts, process maps. The consultant's role shifts from producer to director. Consultants who stay in producer mode lose the efficiency benefit and compromise their ability to validate what's been generated.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
Intent WritingEvery agent-assisted design artifact starts with an intent statement. Quality here determines whether output is usable or requires complete regeneration.
Output ValidationArchitecture sign-off is a formal gate. Validation must be structured and documented — not an impression formed on visual review.
Human-AI OrchestrationThe consultant must decide what agents design, what humans design, and how closely to supervise the blend.
DiscernmentAgent-generated architecture can look complete and be subtly wrong. Calibrated scrutiny is required when output is polished.
Gate Question

Was the architecture reviewed against defined criteria — and did the customer actively participate in shaping it, or only approve it?

QL Playbook — What's Happening Here

Phase 2 in the QL playbook is where agent-assisted design begins in earnest — the IA uses customer-confirmed Intent Statements as the brief for architecture generation. The AO configures the build environment and ensures connectors are operational. The Govern moment (M17) runs continuously from here: non-functional acceptance criteria are expressed in Intent Statements so the Pipeline Gate can enforce them automatically. The IA integrates specialist sign-offs rather than personally validating every dimension — the skill demand is knowing what requires specialist review vs. what the IA can own.

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 3

Architecture sign-off is the last gate before delivery velocity kicks in. Any ambiguity in the architecture compounds at Phase 3 speed. Before Phase 3 opens, confirm that every agent-facing component has a clear, specific intent statement attached to it.

Phase 3 Deliver Gate: Approved Release Plan
Landing — What to Arrive With

This is the highest-velocity phase in an AI-native model. Agents are building continuously. The consultant's primary job is not construction — it is orchestration, discernment, and continuous validation across a fast-moving, high-volume build cycle. This is the phase most likely to expose automation complacency.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
DiscernmentAgents produce plausible-looking output at speed. Calibrated vigilance — not just accepting work that appears complete — is the primary skill demand.
Output ValidationAt delivery velocity, validation must be fast AND systematic. Continuous validation loops built into the build cycle, not tacked on at the end.
Human-AI OrchestrationBuild tasks vary enormously in how safely they can be delegated. Calibrated judgment must be maintained across the full build cycle.
Intent WritingNew sub-tasks surface throughout delivery. Intent is not set once at kickoff — it must be iteratively refined as the build evolves.
Gate Question

Does the release plan reflect what was actually built — was each component validated against the intent and outcomes defined in Phases 0 and 1 — and have responsible AI risks been actively reviewed?

QL Playbook — What's Happening Here

M07 Build Day Loop: the core delivery rhythm. Each day produces approved, deployed, demoed work — no bypass on the Pipeline Gate. M08 Daily Demo: working software in front of the customer every day; this is also the primary continuous validation loop. M09 Pipeline Gate: automated gate wired to the GitHub repo; no build artifact advances without passing it. M17 Govern runs alongside — the IA integrates automated gates and specialist sign-offs rather than personally validating everything. Call-Transcript Mining (M18) runs continuously: Gemini transcribes every customer session; mining agents emit Decision and risk candidates for the IA to review within 24h.

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 4

Before Phase 4 opens, confirm that UAT criteria trace directly back to the success criteria defined in Phase 0. If they don't, UAT will test the wrong things and the Go/No-Go decision will be made on incomplete evidence.

Phase 4 Deploy Gate: UAT Sign-Off + Formal Go/No-Go
Landing — What to Arrive With

Deployment is the point of irreversibility. The Go/No-Go is the highest-stakes judgment call in the lifecycle — and in an AI-native model, the consultant is forming that judgment about work that was largely agent-generated. This gate is where discernment is most consequential and most likely to fail under stakeholder pressure.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
DiscernmentThe Go/No-Go requires a clear, defensible judgment about whether the system is ready — not whether it looks ready or whether stakeholders are ready to move on.
Output ValidationUAT criteria must trace to the Phase 0 success criteria. If they don't, UAT is testing the wrong things.
Change Co-CreationUAT with genuine customer participation reduces post-go-live adoption friction significantly.
Gate Question

Can the consultant make a defensible, evidence-based case that this system is ready to deliver the outcomes defined in Phase 0 — and does the customer share that confidence?

QL Playbook — What's Happening Here

M11 UAT Cycle: customer-owned validation — the pod supports, the customer drives. Defects classify and route back through the Build Day Loop. M12 UAT Sign-off: the formal gate that closes Phase 4. The Go/No-Go is a PL judgment call made against the UAT Sign-off artifact; it is the highest-stakes human decision in the engagement lifecycle. The pilot tracking tool (Alex Killough) surfaces time-in-phase data and phase status at this gate — the consultant's judgment must integrate that data, not just the completion checklist.

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 5

Go-live is not the end. In an AI-native model, agents can continue optimizing in production. Before Phase 5 opens, confirm that the customer team knows what decisions they are now responsible for making — and that they have the capability to make them.

Phase 5 Adopt/Consume Gate: Release Notes + Customer-Accepted Handoff Package
Landing — What to Arrive With

Phase 5 in an AI-native model is a transfer of governance responsibility, not a training event. A customer team that cannot make sound human-AI orchestration decisions after the consultant leaves is not ready for handoff, regardless of what the documentation says.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
Outcome FramingAdoption is only meaningful measured against the outcomes defined in Phase 0. The handoff package must include a measurement plan, not just system documentation.
Human-AI OrchestrationThe customer's team now owns the orchestration decisions the consultant was making. That capability must be transferred deliberately.
Change Co-CreationCustomers who co-created the solution drive their own adoption. Phase 5 reinforces that ownership.
Gate Question

Does the customer have everything they need to measure outcomes, maintain the system, and govern their own human-AI orchestration decisions — without the consultant in the room?

QL Playbook — What's Happening Here

M13 Go-Live: production deploy. M14 Hypercare: post-go-live support period. M15 Retro: structured retrospective — the QL playbook requires write-back to Engagement Memory; every engagement contributes to Practice Memory, consumption-only is a methodology violation. M16 Seal Engagement: Engagement Memory becomes immutable; the engagement formally closes. The Intelligence Layer Loop (M20) runs alongside — patterns from this engagement feed directly into prompt refinements and reusable assets for the next team. The exit interview (Stephanie's pilot mechanism) runs at Phase 5 close: 20 minutes with the PM and one senior consultant.

Takeoff — Carry This Beyond the Engagement

Document two things before you close: one intent statement that worked well enough to reuse, and one validation decision that revealed something about what good looks like on this engagement. These become reusable patterns for the next team.

Enablement Approach

Three deliverables that work alongside the operational infrastructure already being built — not competing with it.

Where This Work Sits

Rohit's site covers the execution layer: what to do, who does it, which tools, in what sequence. Stephanie and Amy cover operational onboarding: how to get stood up on a pilot engagement. This work is the layer in between — the skills and behaviors that determine whether a consultant can actually execute the playbook well under agentic conditions.

1 Skills & Behaviors Bridge Slack Canvas

Not an orientation — that exists. This is the connector that ties the operational onboarding to the human layer. Once a consultant understands the model and the playbook, this piece answers: here are the specific skills and behaviors that make it work — and the ones that break it when skipped. Short, designed to hook into what Rohit and Stephanie have already built, not to replace it.

Connects to: Rohit's execution playbook and Stephanie/Amy's onboarding — this is the bridge between operational knowledge and behavioral readiness
2 Gate-by-Gate Skills & Behaviors Slack Canvas

The core deliverable. One card per phase (Pre-Flight through Phase 5), covering: what to land with, the skills load-bearing at that gate, the gate question, and what to carry forward. Not a checklist of activities — a judgment prompt for the moments right before and after each formal gate decision. Distinct from Rohit's playbook because it names the human capability layer at each stop, not the operational steps.

Priority job aids — Intent Writing and Output Validation canvases — attach to this as phase-specific references
3 Delivery Model Contingency Slack Canvas

What to do when AI-native isn't working and you need to shift delivery models. The three-tier model (Traditional → AI-Augmented → AI-Native) means a consultant may need to step down mid-engagement. This piece covers: the signals that indicate a shift is needed, who makes that call and how, what changes operationally when you shift, and what changes behaviorally. This is a pure judgment case that exists nowhere in the execution playbook — and it's high-stakes because the wrong call in either direction is costly.

Completely uncovered by the existing infrastructure — the most distinct contribution this layer makes

What Each Piece Is Not

Skills & Behaviors Bridge

Not an MVG orientation. Not a repeat of the playbook. The orientation exists — this hooks into it and shifts from what the model is to what it demands of you.

Gate-by-Gate Guide

Not a checklist of gate artifacts. Rohit's site has that. This is the human judgment layer at each gate — the skills that are load-bearing and what breaks when they're skipped.

Delivery Model Contingency

Not a failure document. Knowing when and how to shift delivery models is a professional judgment skill — the same kind of calibration that defines a senior consultant.

Data Collection Mechanisms

Five mechanisms run in parallel. Together they cover real-time signal, gate-level reflection, post-project depth, pattern synthesis, and field observation — the full picture needed to design the three-track program that follows the pilot.

1 Feedback Loop — Always On Delivery Governance / Lauren Hunt

Async, low-friction, continuous. Consultants drop skill observations into #ql-learnings-feedback in the flow of work. A habit prompt pinned to the channel: "What was hard? What did you wish you had?" No structure required.

Feeds: Which job aids are missing or insufficient at the Base Layer
2 Gate Pulse Check — At Each Submission Alex Killough / Victoria Gailey

3–5 structured questions tied to each gate artifact submission. Piggybacks on the ~30 min/week PM tracking cadence — no new submission event. Questions vary by phase and target the load-bearing skills at that gate. The tracking tool is live: Alex Killough has shipped the first version — PM View for project managers to submit phase data, Governance/Admin View for the pilot team to monitor. As PM submissions come in, data populates the governance view in real time. Skills observation questions should be added to the PM View form to capture this signal alongside the operational data.

Feeds: Which skills are actually load-bearing at each phase vs. which are theoretical
3 Exit Interview — At Project Close Stephanie Flasch / Delivery Governance

20-minute structured conversation with the PM and one senior consultant per project at Phase 5 handoff. Three questions: What was hardest? What would you have wanted earlier? What did you invent that should be captured? The richest source for full program design.

Feeds: What the experience arc of the Proficiency Track should feel like
4 Cohort Pattern Digest — Weekly Delivery Governance / Lauren Hunt

The Delivery Governance Team is already planning a weekly cohort digest. A simple "skill observation" field added to the digest format lets consultants narrate what they're noticing about their own capability gaps in context. Low lift to add; high signal for L&D.

Feeds: Language and framing that resonates with consultants in the field
5 AI Coach Observations — Weekly PACE Team / Kristen Lappin

For pilot projects with a designated Regional AI Coach or PACE team member, a simple weekly observation template: What skill gap did you observe? What did you do about it? Field-level behavioral data that self-report cannot produce.

Feeds: What the Immersive Cohort mock project needs to simulate to be realistic
