Why This Exists

The QL + MVG pilot has a governance layer, a metrics layer, and a tooling layer. What it doesn't have yet is a skills layer — the enablement that helps consultants understand why the gates exist, what judgment they require at each stop, and how to navigate them with confidence rather than compliance.

Stephanie flagged this directly: consultants will need enablement on the MVG model itself, not just on how to deliver the work. This proposal addresses that gap.

The "Yes And" Structure

This pilot enablement is Track 1 — minimum viable, June 1 ready. The full three-track program (Base Layer, Proficiency Track, Immersive Cohort) is Track 2, built for scale after the pilot proves the model. Nothing here is disposable — the gate cards, job aids, and data collection mechanisms all become inputs to the full program design.

MVG Orientation Brief

For consultants onboarding to a QL + MVG pilot project.

The Minimum Viable Governance framework is not a compliance layer. It is the structure that makes AI-native delivery repeatable rather than lucky. MVG gives the engagement its spine — a set of phase gates where humans make deliberate decisions before work advances. Without it, agentic delivery is fast but ungoverned. With it, speed and rigor coexist.

The Governing Logic

Agents execute quickly, plausibly, and without self-doubt — the gates are where humans catch what agents cannot.

What Changes in AI-Native Delivery

  • Build compresses — agents do construction work that previously took weeks
  • Intent Definition becomes a first-class phase — what you ask agents to do matters more than how fast they do it
  • Refinement and Validation become the new critical path — a continuous loop, not a phase-end check
  • The gates become more consequential — errors amplified by agent velocity are expensive to catch late

What Stays the Same

  • The customer still needs to own the outcome
  • The consultant is still accountable for quality and fit
  • The engagement still fails if the wrong thing is built fast
Human-led, agent-powered

Agents accelerate execution. Consultants lead strategy, validate output, and govern the decisions that cannot be automated.

Gate-by-Gate Flight Guide

Each gate card covers the full stop: what to land with, the skills that are load-bearing, the gate decision, and what to carry into the next phase.

Pre-Flight Before the Engagement Begins
The One Question That Determines Everything

Is this customer actually ready for AI-native delivery? Before Phase 0 opens, the consultant must assess whether the engagement should proceed under an AI-native model at all. The Quantum Leap program defines five readiness conditions: asset and data readiness, governance orientation, rapid decisioning capability, willingness to experiment, and contracts in order.

Skill in Focus
Customer AI Readiness Assessment
Phase 0 Internal Onboarding Gate: Validated Success Criteria
Landing — What to Arrive With

Before this gate closes, the team needs a shared, measurable definition of success in outcome terms, not deliverable terms. The success criteria validated here are the quality standard every downstream gate will be measured against. If they're vague now, every gate that follows has no teeth.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
Outcome FramingSuccess criteria must describe what changes for the customer — not what gets built.
Intent WritingThe Phase 1 roadmap is grounded in intent established here. Vague intent at Phase 0 produces unpredictable agent behavior downstream.
Data Quality JudgmentBefore agents are scoped for any task, the data must be assessed as fit for agentic use — not just available.
Gate Question

Does the team have a shared, measurable definition of success — and has the customer's data been assessed as fit for the agentic workflows being planned?

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 1

The success criteria validated here are the anchor for every epic in the Phase 1 roadmap. Take them into the first customer session and make them visible. If a proposed epic can't be traced to one of these criteria, that's a scope question — not a delivery task.

Phase 1 Prepare Gate: Customer-Approved Engagement Roadmap (Epics)
Landing — What to Arrive With

The roadmap the customer approves here must be one they helped build — not one they were asked to sign off on. A roadmap the customer co-authored creates ownership. A roadmap the customer approved creates a future scope dispute.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
Change Co-CreationThe roadmap must be co-authored in working sessions, not presented for approval.
Outcome FramingEach epic should trace to an outcome, not a feature. The roadmap is a value realization plan, not a task list.
Intent WritingCustomer-approved epics become the primary input to agent-assisted design in Phase 2. Imprecise epics produce unpredictable agent output.
Systems ThinkingThe roadmap should reflect how the customer's systems, data, and people interact — not just what the software will do.
Gate Question

Would a team member joining mid-stream — or an agent acting on this roadmap — understand exactly what success looks like for each epic, and is the scope bounded enough to deliver it?

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 2

The epics approved here become the design brief for Phase 2. Before agents begin generating architecture artifacts, each epic needs a well-formed intent statement — not just an epic label. The first task of Phase 2 is translating customer-approved epics into agent-ready intent.

Phase 2 Design Gate: Architecture Sign-Off
Landing — What to Arrive With

Agents begin doing real construction in this phase — generating solution designs, architecture artifacts, process maps. The consultant's role shifts from producer to director. Consultants who stay in producer mode lose the efficiency benefit and compromise their ability to validate what's been generated.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
Intent WritingEvery agent-assisted design artifact starts with an intent statement. Quality here determines whether output is usable or requires complete regeneration.
Output ValidationArchitecture sign-off is a formal gate. Validation must be structured and documented — not an impression formed on visual review.
Human-AI OrchestrationThe consultant must decide what agents design, what humans design, and how closely to supervise the blend.
DiscernmentAgent-generated architecture can look complete and be subtly wrong. Calibrated scrutiny is required when output is polished.
Gate Question

Was the architecture reviewed against defined criteria — and did the customer actively participate in shaping it, or only approve it?

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 3

Architecture sign-off is the last gate before delivery velocity kicks in. Any ambiguity in the architecture compounds at Phase 3 speed. Before Phase 3 opens, confirm that every agent-facing component has a clear, specific intent statement attached to it.

Phase 3 Deliver Gate: Approved Release Plan
Landing — What to Arrive With

This is the highest-velocity phase in an AI-native model. Agents are building continuously. The consultant's primary job is not construction — it is orchestration, discernment, and continuous validation across a fast-moving, high-volume build cycle. This is the phase most likely to expose automation complacency.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
DiscernmentAgents produce plausible-looking output at speed. Calibrated vigilance — not just accepting work that appears complete — is the primary skill demand.
Output ValidationAt delivery velocity, validation must be fast AND systematic. Continuous validation loops built into the build cycle, not tacked on at the end.
Human-AI OrchestrationBuild tasks vary enormously in how safely they can be delegated. Calibrated judgment must be maintained across the full build cycle.
Intent WritingNew sub-tasks surface throughout delivery. Intent is not set once at kickoff — it must be iteratively refined as the build evolves.
Gate Question

Does the release plan reflect what was actually built — was each component validated against the intent and outcomes defined in Phases 0 and 1 — and have responsible AI risks been actively reviewed?

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 4

Before Phase 4 opens, confirm that UAT criteria trace directly back to the success criteria defined in Phase 0. If they don't, UAT will test the wrong things and the Go/No-Go decision will be made on incomplete evidence.

Phase 4 Deploy Gate: UAT Sign-Off + Formal Go/No-Go
Landing — What to Arrive With

Deployment is the point of irreversibility. The Go/No-Go is the highest-stakes judgment call in the lifecycle — and in an AI-native model, the consultant is forming that judgment about work that was largely agent-generated. This gate is where discernment is most consequential and most likely to fail under stakeholder pressure.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
DiscernmentThe Go/No-Go requires a clear, defensible judgment about whether the system is ready — not whether it looks ready or whether stakeholders are ready to move on.
Output ValidationUAT criteria must trace to the Phase 0 success criteria. If they don't, UAT is testing the wrong things.
Change Co-CreationUAT with genuine customer participation reduces post-go-live adoption friction significantly.
Gate Question

Can the consultant make a defensible, evidence-based case that this system is ready to deliver the outcomes defined in Phase 0 — and does the customer share that confidence?

Takeoff — Carry This Into Phase 5

Go-live is not the end. In an AI-native model, agents can continue optimizing in production. Before Phase 5 opens, confirm that the customer team knows what decisions they are now responsible for making — and that they have the capability to make them.

Phase 5 Adopt/Consume Gate: Release Notes + Customer-Accepted Handoff Package
Landing — What to Arrive With

Phase 5 in an AI-native model is a transfer of governance responsibility, not a training event. A customer team that cannot make sound human-AI orchestration decisions after the consultant leaves is not ready for handoff, regardless of what the documentation says.

Skills Load-Bearing at This Gate
Outcome FramingAdoption is only meaningful measured against the outcomes defined in Phase 0. The handoff package must include a measurement plan, not just system documentation.
Human-AI OrchestrationThe customer's team now owns the orchestration decisions the consultant was making. That capability must be transferred deliberately.
Change Co-CreationCustomers who co-created the solution drive their own adoption. Phase 5 reinforces that ownership.
Gate Question

Does the customer have everything they need to measure outcomes, maintain the system, and govern their own human-AI orchestration decisions — without the consultant in the room?

Takeoff — Carry This Beyond the Engagement

Document two things before you close: one intent statement that worked well enough to reuse, and one validation decision that revealed something about what good looks like on this engagement. These become reusable patterns for the next team.

Priority Job Aids for Pilot Launch

Three pieces prioritized for June 1 based on consequence on a live engagement and build feasibility. Remaining Base Layer job aids ship in waves after pilot launch, informed by what data collection surfaces.

1
Intent Writing
Slack Canvas

Highest-leverage skill in the framework. Errors in intent propagate through every downstream agent action before anyone notices. Consultants need a reference they can check mid-task.

2
Output Validation
Slack Canvas

Continuous validation loops are new behavior for most consultants. The four-step decision protocol (accept / accept with edits / regenerate / escalate) gives them a decision, not just an instruction.

3
Discernment
Trailhead Unit

Automation complacency is the invisible risk at Phase 3 velocity. Naming it — and explaining why it gets worse as tools improve — is the minimum intervention before consultants hit delivery speed.

Data Collection Mechanisms

Five mechanisms run in parallel. Together they cover real-time signal, gate-level reflection, post-project depth, pattern synthesis, and field observation — the full picture needed to design the three-track program that follows the pilot.

1 Feedback Loop — Always On GT / Lauren Hunt

Async, low-friction, continuous. Consultants drop skill observations into #ql-learnings-feedback in the flow of work. A habit prompt pinned to the channel: "What was hard? What did you wish you had?" No structure required.

Feeds: Which job aids are missing or insufficient at the Base Layer
2 Gate Pulse Check — At Each Submission Victoria Gailey / Michele Lubeck

3–5 structured questions tied to each gate artifact submission. Piggybacks on the ~30 min/week PM tracking cadence GT is already designing — no new submission event. Questions vary by phase and target the load-bearing skills at that gate.

Feeds: Which skills are actually load-bearing at each phase vs. which are theoretical
3 Exit Interview — At Project Close Stephanie Flasch / GT Team

20-minute structured conversation with the PM and one senior consultant per project at Phase 5 handoff. Three questions: What was hardest? What would you have wanted earlier? What did you invent that should be captured? The richest source for full program design.

Feeds: What the experience arc of the Proficiency Track should feel like
4 Cohort Pattern Digest — Weekly GT / Lauren Hunt

GT is already planning a weekly cohort digest. A simple "skill observation" field added to the digest format lets consultants narrate what they're noticing about their own capability gaps in context. Low lift to add; high signal for L&D.

Feeds: Language and framing that resonates with consultants in the field
5 AI Coach Observations — Weekly PACE Team / Kristen Lappin

For pilot projects with a designated Regional AI Coach or PACE team member, a simple weekly observation template: What skill gap did you observe? What did you do about it? Field-level behavioral data that self-report cannot produce.

Feeds: What the Immersive Cohort mock project needs to simulate to be realistic
