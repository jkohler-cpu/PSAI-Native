Design principle: consequences compound

Each act is set at a different point in delivery — so learners feel the stakes change. Mistakes in Act 1 (before the build) are cheap to fix. Mistakes in Act 2 (mid-delivery, under time pressure) are expensive. By Act 3, the consequences of earlier decisions become visible to the customer. The arc is designed so this is felt, not just stated.

Modality stack: three tools, three purposes

Trailhead carries the structured learning spine for each act — concept input, scenario, and checkpoint. Slack always-on canvases serve as the in-the-moment reference layer: decision protocols, anti-patterns, and observable behaviors accessible without leaving the flow of work. Yoodli handles the communicative clusters — Change Co-Creation, Customer AI Readiness conversations, Outcome Framing — where judgment requires a practiced human voice, not just reading comprehension.

Act 1: Before You Build

Phases 0 and 1 — the skills that determine whether the engagement is set up to succeed.

1
Act 1 · Pre-Flight · Phase 0 · Phase 1 · Delivery + GTM shared
Before You Build
The engagement can still be shaped here. Mistakes are cheap to fix now — and expensive after Phase 2 opens. This act puts learners in the Pre-Flight and Phase 0–1 gates: assessing readiness, framing outcomes, surfacing assumptions, and producing the intent that agents will act on.
~7 hrs
Skills covered
Customer AI Readiness Assessment Data Quality Judgment (Data Cloud readiness) Assumption Surfacing & Documentation Outcome Framing Intent Writing + Prompt Template Design

Data Cloud readiness is a Salesforce-specific sub-skill — platform knowledge meeting judgment, not just tool training.

CPS developed
Judgment & Reasoning Systems Thinking Collaborative Communication Information Discernment
Landing — what this act prepares you to arrive with

Before Phase 0 closes, the team needs a shared, measurable definition of success in outcome terms — not deliverable terms. The success criteria validated at the Phase 0 gate are the quality standard every downstream gate will be measured against. Before Phase 1 closes, the roadmap must be one the customer helped build, not one they were asked to sign off on. A co-authored roadmap creates ownership; an approved roadmap creates a future scope dispute.

Load-bearing skills at these gates
Customer AI ReadinessPre-Flight: should this engagement proceed under an AI-native model at all?
Outcome FramingPhase 0: success criteria must describe what changes for the customer — not what gets built.
Data Quality JudgmentPhase 0: data must be assessed as fit for agentic use — not just available.
Change Co-CreationPhase 1: roadmap built in working sessions with the customer, not presented for approval.
Intent WritingPhase 1: customer-approved epics become the brief for agent-assisted design in Phase 2. Imprecise epics produce unpredictable agent output.

Gate questions — the scenarios put learners here
  • Pre-FlightIs this customer actually ready for AI-native delivery — and if not, what's the honest recommendation?
  • Phase 0Does the team have a shared, measurable definition of success — and has the customer's data been assessed as fit for the agentic workflows being planned?
  • Phase 1Would a team member joining mid-stream — or an agent acting on this roadmap — understand exactly what success looks like for each epic, and is the scope bounded enough to deliver it?
What breaks when these skills are skipped
  • Customer AI Readiness skipped— team commits to AI-native delivery with a customer who cannot sustain it; gaps surface when velocity is highest
  • Outcome Framing skipped— success criteria are output-framed; Phase 1 roadmap has no outcome anchor; every downstream gate has no teeth
  • Intent Writing skipped— imprecise epics produce unpredictable agent behavior at Phase 2 velocity; errors are expensive to catch late
  • Change Co-Creation skipped— roadmap is presented for approval, not co-authored; scope creep begins before delivery starts
Takeoff — what learners carry into Act 2

The success criteria and intent statements produced here are the quality standard every Phase 2 and 3 gate will be measured against. Act 2 opens at architecture sign-off — the last gate before delivery velocity kicks in. Any ambiguity in intent established in Act 1 compounds at that speed.

Act 2: While You're Delivering

Phases 2 and 3 — the skills that operate at delivery velocity, under the exact conditions where they most often fail.

2
Act 2 · Phase 2 · Phase 3 · Delivery path
While You're Delivering
This act deliberately introduces time pressure and volume — the conditions under which automation complacency, validation shortcuts, and orchestration failures actually happen. Architecture sign-off through the Build Day Loop: agents are doing real construction, and the consultant's job is direction, discernment, and continuous validation — not production.
~8 hrs
Skills covered
Agentforce Agent Design Principles Failure Mode Literacy (Agentforce, Claude, Cursor) Human-AI Orchestration Discernment Output Validation Responsible AI Sensing Einstein Trust Layer & Data Governance

Failure Mode Literacy is tool-specific — Agentforce, Claude, and Cursor each have distinct failure patterns that need to be recognized before they cause customer-visible problems.

CPS developed
Judgment & Reasoning Information Discernment Adaptive Learning Creative Direction & Taste
Landing — what this act prepares you to arrive with

Phase 2 opens with agents beginning real construction — generating solution designs, architecture artifacts, process maps. The consultant's role shifts from producer to director. Phase 3 is the highest-velocity phase: agents building continuously, the consultant orchestrating and validating across a fast-moving build cycle. This act is where automation complacency is most likely to take hold.

Load-bearing skills at these gates
Intent WritingEvery agent-assisted design artifact starts with an intent statement. Quality here determines whether output is usable or requires complete regeneration.
Output ValidationArchitecture sign-off is a formal gate. Validation must be structured and documented — not an impression formed on visual review. At Phase 3 velocity, it must be fast AND systematic.
DiscernmentAgent-generated architecture can look complete and be subtly wrong. Calibrated vigilance is required when output is polished — especially at delivery velocity.
Human-AI OrchestrationBuild tasks vary enormously in how safely they can be delegated. Calibrated judgment must be maintained across the full build cycle, not just at the start.

Gate questions — the scenarios put learners here
  • Phase 2Was the architecture reviewed against defined criteria — and did the customer actively participate in shaping it, or only approve it?
  • Phase 3Does the release plan reflect what was actually built — was each component validated against the intent and outcomes defined in Phases 0 and 1 — and have responsible AI risks been actively reviewed?
What breaks when these skills are skipped
  • Discernment skipped— agent-generated architecture looks complete and is subtly wrong; errors pass through at Phase 3 velocity and become expensive
  • Output Validation skipped— validation is impressionistic; automation complacency sets in; consultant loses situation awareness of what was built and why
  • Human-AI Orchestration skipped— build tasks delegated without calibration; consultant finds out at Phase 4 that critical components were handled incorrectly at Phase 3 speed
  • Intent Writing skipped mid-build— new sub-tasks scoped imprecisely; intent is treated as set-once at kickoff rather than iteratively refined as the build evolves
Takeoff — what learners carry into Act 3

Architecture sign-off and the release plan are the last human-governed gates before the customer sees what was built in UAT. Act 3 opens at deployment — the point of irreversibility. Confirm before moving that UAT criteria trace back to the Phase 0 success criteria. If they don't, UAT will test the wrong things.

Act 3: With the Customer & Beyond

Phases 4, 5, and reuse — where the consequences of earlier decisions become visible, and the learning compounds into the next engagement.

3
Act 3 · Phase 4 · Phase 5 · Reuse · Delivery path
With the Customer & Beyond
Deployment is the point of irreversibility. The consequences of every earlier decision become visible here — in UAT, in the Go/No-Go, in whether the customer can govern their own system after handoff. This act also establishes the habit of writing back to practice memory so what was learned on this engagement compounds into the next one.
~6 hrs
Skills covered
Change Co-Creation & Scope Governance Under Acceleration Communicating AI-Generated Work Consumption Model Literacy HOK-to-Agentic Transition Judgment Field Pattern Documentation

Scope Governance Under Acceleration — when AI makes everything feel fast and possible, scope expands faster and less visibly. This act teaches holding the boundary without killing momentum.

CPS developed
Collaborative Communication Creative Direction & Taste Adaptive Learning Systems Thinking
Landing — what this act prepares you to arrive with

The Go/No-Go is the highest-stakes judgment call in the engagement lifecycle — and in an AI-native model, the consultant is forming that judgment about work that was largely agent-generated. Phase 5 is not a training event; it is a transfer of governance responsibility. A customer team that cannot make sound human-AI orchestration decisions after the consultant leaves is not ready for handoff, regardless of what the documentation says.

Load-bearing skills at these gates
DiscernmentPhase 4: the Go/No-Go requires a clear, defensible judgment about whether the system is ready — not whether it looks ready or whether stakeholders are ready to move on.
Output ValidationPhase 4: UAT criteria must trace to the Phase 0 success criteria. If they don't, UAT is testing the wrong things.
Change Co-CreationPhase 4–5: UAT with genuine customer participation reduces post-go-live adoption friction. Customers who co-created the solution drive their own adoption.
Outcome FramingPhase 5: the handoff package must include a measurement plan, not just system documentation. Adoption is only meaningful measured against Phase 0 outcomes.
Human-AI OrchestrationPhase 5: the customer's team now owns the orchestration decisions the consultant was making. That capability must be transferred deliberately.

Gate questions — the scenarios put learners here
  • Phase 4Can the consultant make a defensible, evidence-based case that this system is ready to deliver the outcomes defined in Phase 0 — and does the customer share that confidence?
  • Phase 5Does the customer have everything they need to measure outcomes, maintain the system, and govern their own human-AI orchestration decisions — without the consultant in the room?
What breaks when these skills are skipped
  • Discernment skipped at Go/No-Go— decision made on completion rate rather than outcome-readiness; known issues rationalized under deployment pressure
  • Outcome Framing skipped at handoff— handoff documents what was built but not how to measure whether it worked; customer cannot tell if the engagement succeeded
  • Human-AI Orchestration not transferred— customer cannot govern their own agents after the consultant leaves; adoption stalls or degrades without support
  • Pattern contribution skipped— engagement memory not written back; next team inherits the same gaps; practice never compounds
Takeoff — what learners carry beyond the engagement

Document two things before closing: one intent statement that worked well enough to reuse, and one validation decision that revealed something about what good looks like on this engagement. These are the Field Playbook entries that become reusable patterns for the next team — and the first contribution to the practice memory the whole program depends on.

GTM Path

After completing Act 1 with the delivery track, GTM sellers diverge into a dedicated module — scoping integrity, delivery literacy, and the handoff.

G
GTM Module · After Act 1
Scoping for What Delivery Can Actually Deliver
The GTM path starts from the same place as delivery — Act 1's pre-engagement skills — then asks a different question: what does a seller need to understand about AI-native delivery in order to scope it honestly? The module covers delivery literacy, scoping integrity under commercial pressure, and the GTM-to-delivery handoff as a structured skill rather than an informal pass-off.
~3 hrs
Skills covered
MVG delivery literacy — what the phases actually require Scoping integrity under deal pressure Customer AI Readiness — honest pre-sale assessment Outcome Framing in the deal cycle Solutioning Agent fluency GTM-to-delivery handoff protocol

The core tension this module holds: commercial pressure pushes toward yes; delivery integrity requires honesty about readiness. Sellers who understand what delivery phases 0–3 actually require can have a different kind of scoping conversation — and hand off with the information the delivery team needs, not just a signed SOW.

The handoff brief — the GTM path's closing artifact

Instead of a Field Playbook entry, the GTM path closes with a handoff brief: a structured document capturing the customer's assessed readiness, the outcome framing agreed with the sponsor, known data and governance gaps, and the commercial commitments that will constrain delivery. This goes to the delivery team at project kickoff — it's the GTM path's contribution to the shared throughline.

Throughline Mechanisms

The elements that hold the arc together across all five parts — not activities within a single act, but structural connectors across the whole experience.

Throughlines
The Field Playbook
Each act ends with a Playbook entry — a personal artifact documenting what the learner decided, why, and what they'd do differently. By the Close, the Playbook is a complete record of field judgment across all three delivery phases.
Opening & Closing Diagnostic
The same diagnostic runs at the start (Opening) and the end (Close). The comparison makes belief change visible — not just whether the learner passed, but where their mental model shifted and where it didn't.
Pattern Contribution
Act 3 ends with a contribution to the shared PS field knowledge base. Each cohort adds to it. Over time this becomes the Salesforce-specific evidence the skill cluster research currently flags as missing.
Peer Exchange (Act 2 midpoint)
The one synchronous touchpoint in the track. Timed to land at the highest-pressure point — mid-delivery, when validation shortcuts are most tempting — so peer norms around quality are reinforced exactly when they're most needed.
What this track doesn't include

The Proficiency Track is designed to be completable independently, on an active engagement, without requiring cohort scheduling. It does not include live facilitated workshops, embedded coaching, or simulated customer engagement sessions — those belong in the Immersive Cohort (Tier 3). Yoodli covers practice for communicative skills, but it is self-paced; it is not a coached live session. The Immersive Cohort is what happens when you need the full facilitated, live version.
