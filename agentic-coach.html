What You Own

One paragraph. Everything else in this canvas elaborates on it.

You are the accountable driver of Quantum Leap AI-native execution on your pilot team. The execution model is new to everyone — including you. That's fine. What's not optional is execution: daily demos happen, intent statements get written, the Pipeline Gate doesn't get bypassed. When the team wants to default to the old way, you hold the line. When something is broken, you fix it or escalate fast. You are the first escalation point on your team and a direct contributor to the data that determines how this scales. Your execution here is a direct contribution to Professional Services' FY27 V2MOM on AI-native delivery.

→ Problem Solving over Inquisitive Questioning — Gather data through questions to inform solutions you will create and own
→ Ownership over Suggestion — Directly drive initiatives and own accountability for outcomes — not recommendations
→ Outcomes over Activities — Success is measured by business value delivered, not training sessions conducted
→ Commitment over Comfort — Keep the team executing the new execution model even when it's harder than the old way
→ Intervene Early, Escalate Fast — When blockers emerge or the team wants to compromise, act immediately
35% of your time

Driving QL Execution — intent statements, daily demos, non-negotiables enforcement, execution model adherence

30% of your time

Tracking Productivity & Output — dashboard maintenance, metrics analysis, productivity reporting, business value outcome documentation

20% + 15%

Your First Week

The goal is simple: understand your team's starting point and establish your presence before delivery velocity kicks in.

Day 1 Land and assess
  • Attend or review the M01 Handoff — understand what Scopezilla produced and what the pod inherited
  • Introduce yourself to the full pod; be explicit about your role — driver, not just advisor
  • Confirm tool access is complete for every team member: Claude, Cursor, Scopezilla. Flag any gaps immediately.
  • Confirm the AI Tool Consent Canvas is signed — no one touches a customer org without it
Days 2–3 Watch mobilization, read the team
  • Sit in on M02 Mobilization — watch how the team absorbs the pre-sales material and augments the gap list
  • Take note of who's leaning in vs. who's waiting to be told what to do — this is your first read on where resistance will come from
  • Confirm GitHub repo and build sandbox are operational; confirm MeshMesh status for your team (still in procurement for some — treat as a risk until confirmed)
Days 4–5 Review, baseline, brief
  • Review the draft Engagement Roadmap before it goes to the customer — does it describe outcomes or features?
  • Check that intent statements being written are structured (goal, constraints, success criteria, edge cases, guardrails) — not just prompts
  • Set up your productivity tracking dashboard baseline
  • Brief project leadership on your initial read: team readiness, tool gaps, any early risks

Your Weekly Rhythm

What you do every day, every week, and at key milestones.

Daily
  • Confirm the Daily Demo is happening — working software, not a status update
  • Scan the build cycle for automation complacency: is the team accepting agent output without reviewing it?
  • Check the Pipeline Gate — no build artifact advances without passing it
Weekly
  • Deliver productivity report to project leadership: intent completions, demo days, AI usage, blockers, trends
  • Check in with project leadership on adoption progress and team performance
  • Run your coaching interventions based on data — who needs support, what pattern is emerging
Bi-weekly
  • Sync with other Agentic Coaches — what did you solve this week, what needs program-level attention
  • Escalate systemic issues (things that can't be fixed at the team level) to program leadership
End of Pilot
  • Comprehensive retrospective with full metrics analysis and recommendations for scaling
  • Exit interview with the Delivery Governance team — 20 minutes with the PM and one senior consultant

The 7 Non-Negotiables

The commitments you enforce. The team does not get to negotiate them down under delivery pressure. Every non-negotiable exists to protect one thing: the customer's ability to go live successfully and trust what was built.

WIP: Definitions below are inferred from the QL playbook and pilot materials. Flag anything that needs correction to program leadership.
1. Demo Daily

Working software reaches a deployable state every week and is demonstrated to the customer every build day — not status decks, not slide updates, working software. Enforced by the Pipeline Gate (automated, wired to the GitHub repo). No build artifact advances without passing it. "We'll catch up next week" is not an acceptable response to a failed gate.

2. Transparent Collaboration

Everything the team builds is visible to the customer in real time — through daily demos, not status decks. Call-transcript mining (Gemini transcribes every customer session) ensures decisions and risks surface within 24 hours. No surprises at go-live that were detectable earlier.

3. AI-Native Development

Agents do construction work. Consultants write intent statements, validate output, and govern decisions — they are not building manually and using AI as a spell-checker. If the team is doing the same work they always did with an AI tool open in another tab, this non-negotiable is being violated.

4. Trust-Driven Development

Quality is built in, not checked at the end. Tests are written before or alongside code, and the Pipeline Gate enforces this automatically. Trust with the customer is earned through consistent, verifiable delivery — not promises. It is not optional on build days.

5. Continuous Feedback

Customer feedback is gathered every build day through the Daily Demo, not saved up for phase reviews. Decisions get documented the same day they're made. The engagement roadmap is a live document, not a static artifact revised once a week.

6. Data-Driven Decisions

Coaching interventions, delivery decisions, and escalations are grounded in productivity data — velocity metrics, AI usage patterns, defect rates. "I think the team is doing well" is not a status update. Numbers are.

7. Executive Alignment

The customer's executive sponsor is actively aligned and engaged — not just named on the org chart. They signed the Engagement Roadmap at M04. They are available to make and hold decisions quickly. If this isn't happening, flag it: misaligned or absent executive sponsorship is one of the clearest early signals that the engagement will stall.

The Skills You're Coaching

Tool fluency is the floor. The harder coaching job is the judgment skills — the ones that break under delivery pressure even when consultants know they matter.

Skill What it looks like when it's absent Your coaching move
Intent Writing Vague prompts, agent output that keeps missing the mark, time spent editing instead of directing "What would you need to see in the output to know it's right? Write that down before you prompt."
Discernment Team accepts polished agent output without reviewing it — automation complacency setting in "How do you know this is right? What would wrong look like?" — especially at Phase 3 velocity
Output Validation Ad hoc spot-checks, different team members applying different quality standards; Feedback Loop queue swelling because rulings aren't documented Ask for a four-ruling call: Approve · Refine · Reject · Escalate — not a vibe. Rejected intents enter a Feedback Loop state and age until re-reviewed; cycle time reflects the real alignment cost. If Rework Rate is above 25%, this is the skill that's broken.
Outcome Framing Epics are feature-labeled, success is measured by tasks completed "What changes for the customer when this epic is done?" If they can't answer, the epic isn't ready.
Human-AI Orchestration Consultant either over-delegates high-stakes decisions or over-supervises low-stakes tasks; Forward Buffer dropping because intent pipeline isn't being managed "What happens if this agent output is wrong? How reversible is it?" — use that to calibrate supervision level. If Forward Buffer drops below 3 days, the orchestration judgment broke upstream — the cause is almost always an intent pipeline problem, not a build problem.
Three failure modes to recognize before they compound

Intent Deficit — Forward Buffer drops below 3 days in the first week of Build. Agents stall; the IA scrambles. Intervene on intent quality before it becomes a schedule problem. Review Lag — Rework Rate above 25% in the first 5 Build days. Agents are finishing faster than the IA can review; the IA is rushing rulings. Intervene on validation discipline now. Scope-Creep — scope-change log not updated in 3+ days during active Build. Customer refinements are accumulating without classification. Intervene before the unclassified delta becomes a commercial problem.

Holding the Line

Resistance rarely sounds like "I don't want to do this." It sounds like reasonable objections under pressure.

"We don't have time for the daily demo today."
Redirect

This is the most common reversion. The daily demo is not optional — it is the primary continuous feedback loop and a non-negotiable. "I understand we're under pressure. What's the minimum working piece we can show in 15 minutes? That counts." Hold the line.

"This intent statement process is slowing us down — we'll just prompt directly."
Redirect

This is the AI-augmented trap: reverting to prompting like it's a tool rather than directing like it's a resource. "How long did it take to fix the last output that missed? Spend five minutes on the intent statement first."

"The agent output looks good enough — let's move it forward."
Redirect

Automation complacency in real time. "Walk me through how you validated it. What criteria did you use?" Make the validation visible — it short-circuits the shortcut.

"The customer keeps adding scope in the working sessions."
Redirect

Scope expansion disguised as co-creation. Help the team distinguish input that reshapes intent (incorporate it) from input that expands scope (log it, route to a future epic, don't absorb silently). The distinction must be made in the room, in real time.

"We've always done it this way and it works."
Redirect

The clearest reversion signal. "What would 'working' look like under QL? Let's measure it against that." Don't argue about the old way — anchor to the new standard.

Your Peer Coach Community

You are not the only one figuring this out.

Every Agentic Coach on the pilot is in the same position — new execution model, live customer, accountable for making it work. The bi-weekly coach sync is the structured moment to use that.

Bi-weekly Coach Sync
  • Come with: one thing that worked, one thing that broke, one question for the group
  • Share what you solved this week — not just what happened
  • Escalate issues that need program-level attention, not just team-level fixes
Between Syncs — #ql-learnings-feedback
  • Drop observations in the flow of work — not structured, just a habit
  • Prompt: What was hard? What did you wish you had?
  • What you surface here feeds directly into how the execution model scales after the pilot
Why this matters beyond your team

The patterns you surface across syncs and in the feedback channel are the primary input into the three-track learning program that follows this pilot. What you document becomes the playbook for the next cohort. This is not optional overhead — it is the research.
