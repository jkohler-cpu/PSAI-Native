# AI-Native Delivery: Skills Framework

## Purpose

This framework defines the human judgment and professional skills required for consultants staffed on AI-native delivery engagements. It is organized around the **Minimum Viable Governance (MVG) framework** — the operating system that makes AI-native delivery repeatable, accountable, and sustainable at scale.

The PS AI-First Delivery Flywheel identifies **Skilled Talent & Leadership** as a required element for AI-native delivery. This framework is the definition of that element — what skilled talent and leadership actually requires, skill by skill, at each phase of a governed engagement.

It is intended for internal stakeholders and the L&D team designing learning journeys.

**The core question this answers:** At each stage of a governed AI-native engagement, what does a consultant need to be able to DO — and what breaks if they can't?

---

## MVG: The Spine of AI-Native Delivery

The Minimum Viable Governance framework is a modular 6-phase delivery model designed to bring structure and accountability to agentic engagements without adding friction that negates the speed AI enables. Each phase ends with a formal stage gate — a deliberate human decision point before work advances.

This matters because AI-native delivery without governance is not faster delivery. It is faster drift. Agents execute quickly, plausibly, and without self-doubt. The stage gates are where humans catch what agents cannot: misaligned intent, scope creep, customer readiness gaps, and second-order risks that weren't visible at kickoff.

| Phase | Name | Stage Gate |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Internal Onboarding | Validated Success Criteria |
| 1 | Prepare | Customer-Approved Engagement Roadmap (Epics) |
| 2 | Design | Architecture Sign-Off |
| 3 | Deliver | Approved Release Plan |
| 4 | Deploy | UAT Sign-Off + Formal Go/No-Go |
| 5 | Adopt/Consume | Release Notes + Customer-Accepted Handoff Package |

MVG applies to any execution model — traditional, AI-augmented, or fully agentic. What changes across those models is not the phase structure. What changes is the **human skill demand at each gate.** The more agentic the delivery, the more judgment-intensive each checkpoint becomes.

## Why the Skills Shift: The Quantum Leap Context

Salesforce's Quantum Leap model describes what AI-native delivery looks like when it's working. Understanding it explains why the skills on this list are different from what traditional consulting requires.

| Delivery Model | What it looks like | Skill demand |
|---|---|---|
| **Traditional** | Build is the resource sink. Humans do the construction. | Execution, coordination, technical craft |
| **AI-Augmented** *(the common trap)* | Same engagement shape. AI tools bolted on. Some speed gain, nothing structurally different. | Same as traditional, plus tool fluency |
| **AI-Native** | Agents construct. **Intent Definition** is a first-class phase. **Refinement & Validation** is the new critical path. Consultants are human-led, agent-powered. | Intent writing, discernment, validation, orchestration, systems thinking |

The shift from AI-Augmented to AI-Native is not automatic. Most organizations stop at AI-Augmented and call it transformation. The difference is structural: AI-Native requires redesigning the engagement shape, not just adding tools to the existing one. MVG is what makes that redesign governable. The skills below are what make it executable — and what make Speed with Rigor possible rather than just fast.

The Minimum Viable Governance framework is not a backdrop for this skills framework — it is the organizing logic. Every skill in this document exists because MVG requires it. Every gap in current learning programs is a gap in what consultants can actually do at a specific gate. ### What MVG Is and Why It Matters

MVG is a modular six-phase delivery model designed to bring structure and accountability to agentic engagements without adding friction that negates the speed AI enables. It applies to traditional, AI-augmented, or fully agentic execution. What changes across those models is not the phase structure — it is the **human skill demand at each gate**.

Each phase ends with a formal stage gate: a deliberate human decision point before work advances. These gates are not administrative checkpoints. They are the moments where the engagement is most at risk of going wrong — and where consultant judgment is the only reliable safeguard.

**The more agentic the delivery, the more judgment-intensive each checkpoint becomes.** Agents execute quickly, plausibly, and without self-doubt. The gates are where humans catch what agents cannot: misaligned intent, scope creep, customer readiness gaps, and second-order risks that weren't visible at kickoff. AI-native delivery without MVG is not faster delivery — it is faster drift.

### The Skill Demand Across the Lifecycle

Each MVG phase creates a distinct set of skill demands. What a consultant needs to do well in Phase 0 is different from what Phase 3 requires. The table below shows the primary skill load at each gate — not an exhaustive map, but the judgment calls that most commonly break engagements when they're absent.

| Phase | Gate | Primary Skill Demand | What Breaks Without It |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-Phase 0 | Prerequisite judgment | Customer AI Readiness Assessment | Team commits to AI-native delivery with a customer who cannot sustain it; engagement stalls in Phase 3 when readiness gaps surface |
| Phase 0 | Validated Success Criteria | Outcome Framing + Intent Writing + Data Quality Judgment | Success criteria are output-framed, not outcome-framed; agents are scoped against unassessed data; Phase 1 roadmap has no outcome anchor |
| Phase 1 | Customer-Approved Engagement Roadmap | Change Co-Creation + Outcome Framing + Systems Thinking | Roadmap is presented for approval, not co-authored; epics are feature-labeled; scope creep begins before delivery starts |
| Phase 2 | Architecture Sign-Off | Intent Writing + Output Validation + Discernment | Agent-generated architecture looks complete and is subtly wrong; validation is impressionistic; errors pass through at Phase 3 velocity |
| Phase 3 | Approved Release Plan | Discernment + Output Validation + Human-AI Orchestration | Automation complacency sets in; validation becomes ad hoc; consultant loses situation awareness of what was built and why |
| Phase 4 | UAT Sign-Off + Go/No-Go | Discernment + Output Validation + Change Co-Creation | Go/No-Go is decided on completion rate rather than outcome-readiness; known issues rationalized under deployment pressure |
| Phase 5 | Customer-Accepted Handoff | Outcome Framing + Human-AI Orchestration + Change Co-Creation | Handoff documents what was built but not how to measure whether it worked; customer team cannot govern their own orchestration decisions |

### Why the Skill Demand Intensifies Under Agentic Conditions

In traditional delivery, human review cycles absorb many of the risks the skills above are designed to manage. A developer asks a clarifying question when intent is ambiguous. A PM notices scope creep because they're close to the work. A QA reviewer catches errors because they know the design decisions behind the output.

Agentic delivery removes that absorption layer. The same risks exist — ambiguous intent, scope creep, output errors, readiness gaps — but the mechanisms that caught them informally are gone. Agents don't ask clarifying questions. They don't flag scope expansion. They don't know the design decisions behind their own output.

This is why the skills in this framework are not generic consulting competencies with an AI label added. They are specifically calibrated to the failure modes that agentic delivery introduces:

- **Intent Writing** addresses the failure mode where agents execute on ambiguous specifications without asking
- **Discernment** addresses automation complacency — the well-documented pattern where scrutiny decreases as output quality improves
- **Output Validation** addresses the absence of an author who can explain the design decisions behind a work product
- **Human-AI Orchestration** addresses the calibration challenge of delegating to a system that cannot reliably flag its own uncertainty
- **Systems Thinking** addresses error propagation — the way a flaw in one agent-generated component moves downstream through multiple dependencies before a human detects it
- **Change Co-Creation** addresses the compression of the design-build-present cycle, which eliminates the natural moments where customers would have shaped the work
- **Data Quality Judgment** addresses the amplification of data problems at agent speed and scale
- **Customer AI Readiness Assessment** addresses the commitment risk of scoping AI-native delivery before the conditions that make it viable are confirmed
- **Outcome Framing** addresses the volume problem — when AI can generate deliverables fast, the pressure is to produce more rather than ensure relevance
- **Adaptive Learning** addresses the recalibration requirement — tools evolve faster than any curriculum, and practitioners must be able to update their own approach between formal learning events
- **Creative Direction & Taste** addresses the acceptance problem — when agents produce polished, well-structured output at volume, the default tendency is to accept it without the human judgment layer that distinguishes technically correct from contextually right

### The Common Failure Pattern

Across every skill cluster, the failure mode follows the same logic: **speed creates pressure to skip the human-intensive step the skill is designed to protect.**

Intent writing gets skipped because prompting feels faster. Validation gets skipped because the output looks good. Co-creation gets skipped because the AI already produced something. Readiness assessment gets skipped because the customer asked for AI-native delivery. Data quality gets assumed rather than assessed.

These are not knowledge gaps — they are habits under pressure. Knowing what discernment is does not make a consultant apply it at Phase 3 velocity when a customer is in the room and the release plan is due. That gap — between knowing and doing under real conditions — is precisely what the Proficiency Track and Immersive Cohort are designed to close.

The Base Layer names the skills. The Proficiency Track builds the judgment. The Immersive Cohort stress-tests it. MVG is the through-line that makes all three levels coherent.

## The 11 Core Skill Clusters

These are the human skills that MVG-governed, AI-native delivery requires. Tool fluency is a prerequisite — not a substitute for any of these.

Each skill cluster is grounded in foundational **Core Professional Skills (CPS)** — the human capabilities that become more, not less, important as agents take over execution work. These are: **Judgment & Reasoning**, **Systems Thinking**, **Adaptive Learning**, **Collaborative Communication**, **Information Discernment**, and **Creative Direction & Taste**. They are not a separate track or a parallel framework — they are woven into the definition of each skill below, surfaced in each MVG phase, and developed through the learning experiences in this framework. Each cluster names the CPS it develops so that connection is visible at every level.

**A note on the common failure mode:** In every cluster, the failure mode under AI-native conditions follows the same pattern — *speed creates pressure to skip the human-intensive step the skill is designed to protect.* Intent writing gets skipped because prompting feels faster. Validation gets skipped because the output looks good. Co-creation gets skipped because the AI already produced something. Data quality gets assumed rather than assessed. These are habits under pressure, not just knowledge domains.

### 1. Intent Writing
Translating desired outcomes into precise, structured specifications that agents can act on correctly — including constraints, success criteria, edge cases, and guardrails. The single highest-leverage skill in this framework: errors in intent propagate through every downstream agent action before anyone notices.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* Traditional ambiguity gets caught in human review cycles — a developer asks a clarifying question. Agents don't ask. They execute. The cost of underspecified intent is not a conversation; it is a plausible-looking artifact built on the wrong premise.

*Develops:* **Collaborative Communication** (clarifying and expressing intent precisely), **Creative Direction & Taste** (knowing what good looks like before the agent builds it)

**Sub-skill — Prompt Debugging / Diagnostic Iteration:** When an agent produces bad output, the consultant must diagnose *why* before responding. Was the intent unclear? Was the tool wrong for the task? Was the underlying data bad? Was the output actually fine and the reviewer's criteria were off? These are four different diagnoses that lead to four different responses. Consultants who cannot distinguish them either blame the tool for everything or rewrite prompts when the real issue is data. Microsoft's Copilot deployment teams have specifically named this as a skill distinct from prompt writing — the ability to read a failure and respond to the right cause.

**Sub-skill — Intent Statement vs. User Story Distinction:** An Intent Statement is not a user story with extra steps. A user story describes what a user wants. An Intent Statement specifies what the agent must produce — including outcome, constraints, done criteria, boundaries, and edge cases — with enough precision that there is no ambiguity for an agent to fill with inference. The failure mode: practitioners trained on Agile requirements writing default to user story format, which leaves "done" open to interpretation. Agents don't ask clarifying questions. They execute on what they're given. *Source: Victoria Gailey, MVG Phase Baseline Skills, June 2026.*

---

### 2. Discernment
The capacity to evaluate AI-generated output and make sound, defensible decisions about it — accepting, refining, rejecting, or escalating — even when the output is polished and plausible. The core challenge: not being fooled by fluency.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* Traditional judgment happens in information-sparse environments where you must decide with incomplete data. AI-native judgment happens in information-rich environments where the output *looks complete*. The cognitive risk is the opposite: over-reliance on well-presented but subtly wrong output. This is known as automation complacency — it worsens as tools become more reliable and scrutiny naturally decreases.

*Develops:* **Judgment & Reasoning** (evaluating quality and risk under pressure), **Information Discernment** (filtering signal from plausible-looking noise)

**Sub-skill — Responsible AI Sensing:** Knowing when to flag an AI output, workflow design, or agent behavior because it introduces bias, privacy exposure, or compliance risk — before it becomes a customer problem. This is distinct from output validation, which checks quality against defined criteria. Responsible AI sensing requires the consultant to notice risks that *weren't in the brief*. IBM and Deloitte have both formalized this as a practitioner skill, not just an ethical policy: a consultant reviewing an agent-generated customer segmentation model should be able to recognize when the segmentation criteria could introduce disparate impact, even if the output is technically correct.

**Sub-skill — Design Gap vs. Execution Defect Distinction:** When an agent produces a bad output, the diagnosis determines the response. A design gap means the Intent Statement was flawed — the response is to rewrite the intent. An execution defect means the Intent Statement was sound but the agent failed to execute on it — the response is to refine the prompt or escalate. Conflating the two produces the wrong fix: rewriting a good intent statement wastes a phase; leaving a bad one in place propagates the error through every downstream agent action. In UAT, a design-gap defect is a methodology failure — it means something broke down in Phase 1 or 2 that should have been caught before Build. *Source: Victoria Gailey, MVG Phase Baseline Skills, June 2026.*

---

### 3. Output Validation
A structured, repeatable process for reviewing AI-generated work products against defined quality criteria. Distinct from discernment (in-the-moment judgment) — validation is criterion-based, documented, and tied to a decision protocol: accept / accept with edits / regenerate / escalate. In AI-native delivery, this operates as a **continuous validation loop** built into every phase rather than a QA gate at the end.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* Traditional QA validates work you or your team produced — you know the design decisions behind it. AI-generated work has no author who can explain intent or flag known weaknesses. Validation must be forward-looking (does this meet criteria?) rather than backward-looking (does this match the decisions we made?).

*Develops:* **Judgment & Reasoning** (applying criteria systematically and making defensible decisions), **Information Discernment** (identifying what's accurate, complete, and fit for purpose)

**Sub-skill — Engagement Memory as Validation Record:** In AI-native delivery, every agent output, Trusted Guide decision, customer approval, and scope change should be logged continuously in Engagement Memory. Validation is not just a gate-by-gate event — it is an ongoing audit trail. At handoff, the Engagement Memory is the evidence that every deployed capability traces back to a customer-approved Intent Statement. Consultants who treat validation as a phase-end activity rather than a continuous practice arrive at the handoff without a defensible record. *Source: Victoria Gailey, MVG Phase Baseline Skills, June 2026.*

**Sub-skill — UAT as Confirmation, Not Discovery:** If the daily demo cadence worked throughout Phase 3, UAT should surface no design surprises. The customer has been approving working software daily; UAT is formal confirmation of what they already know. A design-gap defect surfacing in UAT means something failed earlier in the methodology — it is a signal to trigger a Build retrospective, not just a defect to resolve. This reframes how UAT is taught: not as the phase where the customer first sees the solution, but as the phase where they formally accept what they have been shaping all along. *Source: Victoria Gailey, MVG Phase Baseline Skills, June 2026.*

---

### 4. Outcome Framing
Defining and maintaining a shared definition of project success in terms of real business results — changes in behavior, metrics, or strategic position — not deliverables produced or tasks completed. This is the consultant-side practice behind what the PS delivery flywheel calls **Outcome Alignment** — the skill that keeps that flywheel element from being a label rather than a discipline. Harder when AI can generate deliverables faster, because the natural pressure is to produce more rather than ensure relevance.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* When AI compresses delivery, the volume of output goes up. Without disciplined outcome framing, an AI-native engagement can generate impressive documentation and configuration that doesn't move the customer's needle. The consultant's role shifts from "how do I produce this?" to "what should we be producing, and why?" — a harder question that requires more customer-facing courage.

*Develops:* **Judgment & Reasoning** (deciding what matters and why), **Collaborative Communication** (facilitating alignment with customers on what success actually means)

**Sub-skill — Outcome-Traced Artifact Production:** At handoff, every deployed capability should trace back to the Intent Statement that drove it, which traces back to the epic that defined it, which traces back to the success criteria defined in Phase 0. Release Notes are not a summary of what was built — they are an outcome map. Consultants who produce deliverable-framed Release Notes ("Deployed Service Cloud agent") rather than outcome-traced ones ("Agent reduces average handle time — traces to Intent Statement IS-042, Epic E-07, Success Criteria SC-02") leave the customer without a measurement framework and the engagement without a closing loop. *Source: Victoria Gailey, MVG Phase Baseline Skills, June 2026.*

---

### 5. Human-AI Orchestration
Knowing, in real time, when to assign work to an agent, when to do it yourself, when to override an agent, and when an agent's output is ready to move downstream or to a customer. The operating-level skill of managing a human-AI team — not just using AI tools individually.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* Delegating to a human involves assessing their skills, availability, and context. Delegating to an agent involves assessing something different: confidence level, task type, reversibility of output, and proximity to customer-facing consequence. Agents don't push back, don't flag uncertainty reliably, and can be confidently wrong. Calibration is non-obvious and changes as tools evolve.

*Develops:* **Judgment & Reasoning** (making calibrated delegate/supervise/override decisions), **Adaptive Learning** (recalibrating as tools and their capabilities evolve)

**Sub-skill — Customer Influence for Operating Cadence:** AI-native delivery depends on fast customer feedback loops — daily demos, 24–48 hour decision cycles, exec sponsor availability. These are not preferences; they are methodology requirements. If the customer cannot commit to them, the delivery model cannot function as designed. The consultant's job is to establish and maintain these commitments before Phase 0 opens and to re-establish them when they erode. This is distinct from Change Co-Creation (which is about co-designing the solution) — it is about securing the operating conditions that make the methodology possible at all. *Source: Victoria Gailey, MVG Phase Baseline Skills, June 2026.*

---

### 6. Systems Thinking
Understanding the delivery engagement as a system — with feedback loops, interdependencies, and second-order effects — not as a collection of tasks. In agentic contexts this is more urgent than in traditional delivery: one flawed agent output can propagate through multiple downstream agents before a human detects it. Fast-moving systems with opaque feedback loops drift in ways slow-moving human systems do not.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* Traditional systems thinking focuses on human-process interdependencies. AI-native systems thinking must include agents as active participants with their own failure modes — error amplification, context drift, and goal displacement (where an agent optimizes for a proxy metric rather than actual intent). The sociotechnical system is more complex and faster-moving.

*Develops:* **Systems Thinking** (synthesising inputs, recognising upstream/downstream impacts, thinking across workflows), **Judgment & Reasoning** (identifying where human intervention is required before drift becomes visible)

---

### 7. Change Co-Creation
Engaging customers as active co-authors of the delivery process — designing feedback loops where customers shape intent and refine outputs in real time, rather than reacting to finished work. When AI compresses timelines, co-creation is not a collaboration preference; it is a structural requirement. Customers who weren't present for the shaping of work will not own its outcomes.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* Traditional change management is designed for slower cycles — change is introduced after design is finalized. When AI can generate working artifacts in hours, the sequence "design → build → present → react → iterate" collapses. Customers need to be present while the shaping happens, which requires facilitation skills quite different from running a review meeting or delivering a presentation.

*Develops:* **Collaborative Communication** (facilitating real-time alignment across human and AI systems), **Creative Direction & Taste** (reading a room, shaping a session, knowing what input is signal vs. noise)

**Sub-skill — Scope Governance Under Acceleration:** When AI makes everything feel fast and possible, customers get excited and scope expands — faster and less visibly than in traditional delivery. The natural friction of doing the work used to make scope creep detectable. That friction is gone. This sub-skill is specifically about maintaining scope discipline when momentum is high: distinguishing input that should reshape intent from input that represents expansion, and managing the boundary without killing the collaborative energy that co-creation depends on.

---

### 8. Data Quality Judgment
The ability to assess whether a customer's data is actually fit for purpose — complete enough, clean enough, and structured correctly — before committing to an agentic delivery model that depends on it. This is a diagnostic skill that lives upstream of everything else: agents are only as reliable as the data they act on, and a system that works on demo data can fail confidently in production when real data doesn't match the assumptions baked into the intent.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* Traditional delivery could often work around data quality problems through manual workarounds and human judgment. Agentic workflows amplify data problems rather than absorbing them — a bad assumption in the data gets executed at scale and speed before anyone notices. Companies deploying AI in financial services and healthcare have learned this repeatedly: the most common cause of production failure is not a bad model or bad intent, it's data that nobody formally assessed before the engagement was scoped.

*Develops:* **Information Discernment** (assessing source reliability and fitness for purpose), **Systems Thinking** (understanding how data quality propagates through downstream workflows)

*Relationship to other clusters:* This is distinct from systems thinking (which maps how things connect) and from output validation (which reviews what agents produce). Data quality judgment is an earlier, upstream assessment: it determines whether agentic delivery is appropriate at all for a given customer context, and if so, what guardrails must be in place before agents touch production data.

---

### 9. Customer AI Readiness Assessment
The ability to diagnose — before committing to an AI-native delivery model — whether a customer is genuinely ready for it. Salesforce's Quantum Leap program has defined what a ready customer looks like: **asset and data readiness, a governance orientation, rapid decisioning capabilities, willingness to experiment, and contracts in order**. The consultant's job is to assess those conditions honestly before the engagement is scoped — not after delivery stalls. This covers data governance maturity, organizational culture toward AI, process stability, executive sponsorship quality, and the customer team's capacity to co-create and co-govern through delivery. It is a scoping and risk management skill, not a delivery skill.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* The pressure to demonstrate AI-native delivery is high — from customers who want it, from internal incentives to prove the model works, and from the genuine excitement that comes from early proof points. Consultants who skip or rush readiness assessment commit to a delivery model the customer cannot sustain. Accenture and BCG have both identified this as a distinct pre-engagement skill: the ability to have an honest, evidence-based conversation with a customer about their readiness — and to scope a different approach when the answer isn't there yet — without losing the deal or the relationship.

*Develops:* **Judgment & Reasoning** (risk assessment and honest recommendation under commercial pressure), **Collaborative Communication** (navigating a difficult readiness conversation without losing the relationship), **Systems Thinking** (mapping the organizational and technical environment holistically)

*Relationship to other clusters:* Customer AI readiness assessment draws on outcome framing (understanding what the customer is actually trying to achieve) and systems thinking (mapping the organizational environment), but it is distinct because it happens before Phase 0 — it is what determines whether MVG-governed AI-native delivery is the right model for this engagement at all.

---

### 10. Adaptive Learning
The ability to continuously update how you work — experimenting with new workflows, recalibrating mental models as tools evolve, staying effective under conditions that are changing faster than any curriculum can track. In AI-native delivery, the toolset shifts frequently and the right approach to a task this quarter may not be the right approach next quarter. Adaptive learning is what keeps a consultant's capabilities current between formal learning events.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* Traditional professional development happens on predictable cycles — training, certification, practice. AI-native tools evolve on weeks-to-months cycles. A practitioner who waits for formal learning to update their approach will consistently be behind. Adaptive learning requires comfort with experimentation, willingness to be wrong in low-stakes conditions, and a habit of structured reflection that extracts learning from daily work rather than from scheduled programs.

*Develops:* **Adaptive Learning** (curiosity, experimentation, comfort with ambiguity, continuous recalibration of approach)

*Note on development:* This CPS is best developed through the design of learning experiences themselves rather than through a dedicated module. The proficiency track's opening and closing diagnostic, the peer exchange, and the forward brief are all mechanisms for building the reflective habit that underlies adaptive learning. The on-project reinforcement phase of the Immersive Cohort is where it becomes a sustained practice.

---

### 11. Creative Direction & Taste
The ability to apply human perspective, contextual judgment, and a sense of quality to AI-generated work — knowing what good looks like, what feels right for the customer and context, and what an agent has produced that is technically correct but fundamentally off. This is the skill that separates a consultant who directs AI work from one who accepts what the agent produces.

*What makes it harder in an AI-native context:* When agents produce polished, well-structured output at volume, the default tendency is to accept it. Creative direction requires the confidence to say "this is correct but it's not right" — and to articulate why in terms the customer and the team can act on. It also requires knowing when to apply this judgment and when to move on: not everything warrants creative intervention, and over-applying taste to low-stakes outputs wastes the speed AI enables.

*Develops:* **Creative Direction & Taste** (originality, contextual nuance, empathy, judgment about what is meaningful or high quality), **Judgment & Reasoning** (knowing when to intervene and when to let it go)

*Where it surfaces most visibly:* Intent writing (specifying what good looks like before the agent builds it), output validation (applying taste and contextual judgment alongside quality criteria), change co-creation (reading a customer's reaction and knowing what it means), and communicating AI-generated work (deciding how to frame and present work that was largely agent-produced).

## Three Non-Negotiable Mindset Shifts

These are not skills to develop — they are the identity shifts that make the skills above executable. A consultant who knows what discernment is but still thinks of themselves as a builder will default to builder behavior under pressure. These shifts must be internalized before Phase 0. When they are absent, the skills framework is vocabulary without traction.

*Source: Victoria Gailey, MVG Phase Baseline Skills, June 2026.*

---

### 1. Director, Not Builder

**Old:** I spend my day writing configuration, code, and documentation.
**New:** I spend my day writing Intent Statements, reviewing agent output, and making judgment calls.

The failure mode when this shift doesn't happen: practitioners in "builder mode" become bottlenecks. They over-specify Intent Statements — turning them into user stories with implementation detail — or they under-review agent output because reviewing feels like not building. Both behaviors compromise the engagement. In the first case, the consultant is doing the agent's job for it. In the second, the consultant is not doing theirs.

---

### 2. Intent Statements, Not User Stories

**Old:** *"As a supplier manager, I want to see duplicate accounts so I can merge them."*

**New:** *"A user with the Supplier Manager role can identify potential duplicate supplier accounts. The system must surface candidates with >85% match confidence on name, tax ID, and address. It must respect record-level access. It must never auto-merge. Done when the user can review and flag candidates, with all flagged actions logged for audit."*

The failure mode: user stories leave "done" open for interpretation. Intent Statements have done baked in. Agents don't ask clarifying questions — they execute on what they're given. An agent acting on a user story fills the ambiguity with inference. An agent acting on a well-formed Intent Statement produces a predictable, auditable output. The difference between the two is not style — it is whether the consultant is doing the hardest part of their job.

---

### 3. Judgment Over Speed

**Old:** Speed of delivery = how fast I can produce artifacts.
**New:** Speed of delivery = how quickly I make accurate judgment calls that keep agents moving in the right direction.

The failure mode: assuming AI speed means skipping human-intensive steps. A flawed Intent Statement upstream produces plausible-looking artifacts that are wrong all the way down — and at agentic velocity, those artifacts propagate through multiple downstream phases before anyone detects the error. The investment in judgment at the front of each phase is what makes the speed at the back of it safe.

## Skills by MVG Phase

The skills required shift across the delivery lifecycle. Some are load-bearing early; others peak in the middle or at close. What follows maps the primary skill demands at each phase — including what breaks when those skills are absent.

---

### Pre-Phase 0: Before the Engagement Begins
**Not a formal gate — a prerequisite judgment**

Customer AI Readiness Assessment happens before Phase 0 is opened. It determines whether MVG-governed AI-native delivery is the right model for this customer at this time. It is not a sales tool — it is a risk management conversation. Committing to AI-native delivery with a customer who lacks the data maturity, organizational readiness, or executive sponsorship to sustain it produces the worst possible outcome: a fast, well-governed engagement that fails in adoption.

| Skill | Why it matters here | What breaks when it's absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer AI Readiness Assessment** | Establishes whether AI-native delivery is appropriate — and if so, what conditions must be met before agents touch production systems | Team assumes readiness because the customer asked for AI-native delivery; engagement stalls in Phase 3 when data governance gaps or organizational resistance surface |

---

### Phase 0: Internal Onboarding
**Stage Gate: Validated Success Criteria**

This is the pre-customer phase where the delivery team aligns on what success looks like, how the engagement will be governed, and what constraints apply before any agent work begins. It is often treated as administrative. It is not. The success criteria validated here are the quality standard every downstream gate will be measured against.

| Skill | Why it matters at this gate | What breaks when it's absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Outcome Framing** | Success criteria must be defined in outcome terms or the gate has no teeth — a checklist of deliverables is not a success standard | Team lists outputs ("complete discovery doc") instead of outcomes ("customer can demonstrate measurable adoption within 30 days of go-live") |
| **Intent Writing** | The engagement roadmap built in Phase 1 is grounded in intent established here. Vague intent at Phase 0 produces unpredictable agent behavior in Phase 2 and beyond. | Intent is ambiguous ("deliver an Agentforce solution") — no constraints, guardrails, or acceptance criteria; agents fill the gaps with inference |
| **Data Quality Judgment** | Before agents are scoped for any task, the team must assess whether the underlying data is fit for agentic use — not just available | Data quality is assumed based on what the customer says exists; agents execute against incomplete or dirty data; outputs are confidently wrong at scale |
| **Systems Thinking** | The team must understand the customer's system before agents touch it — data readiness, process dependencies, organizational constraints | Team scopes the build without mapping what it connects to; integration failures and adoption gaps surface late |

**Gate question:** Does the team have a shared, measurable definition of success — and has the customer's data been assessed as fit for the agentic workflows being planned?

*CPS in focus at this gate:* Judgment & Reasoning, Systems Thinking, Information Discernment

---

### Phase 1: Prepare
**Stage Gate: Customer-Approved Engagement Roadmap (Epics)**

The team moves from internal alignment to customer alignment. Epics are the customer-approved decomposition of intent into workable units — and this is the first moment where co-creation either happens or doesn't. A roadmap the customer helped build is fundamentally different from a roadmap the customer approved.

| Skill | Why it matters at this gate | What breaks when it's absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Change Co-Creation** | The roadmap must be co-authored, not presented for sign-off. Customers who shaped it stay aligned through delivery. Customers who approved it look for gaps. | Consultant presents a finished roadmap; customer approves without engaging with trade-offs; misalignment surfaces in Phase 3 or 4 as scope disputes |
| **Scope Governance Under Acceleration** | The first co-creation session is also the first moment scope can expand. The consultant must distinguish input that should reshape intent from input that represents expansion — and hold that line without killing the collaborative energy. | Every customer suggestion gets absorbed into the roadmap; scope balloons before delivery begins; the customer is surprised when velocity drops in Phase 3 |
| **Outcome Framing** | Each epic should trace to an outcome, not a feature — the roadmap is a value realization plan, not a task list | Epics are feature-labeled ("Build Service Cloud agent") with no stated outcome; no one can tell whether delivering the epic succeeded |
| **Intent Writing** | Customer-approved epics become the primary input to agent-assisted design in Phase 2. Imprecise epics produce unpredictable agent output. | Epics are broad enough to mean different things to different team members; agents generate designs that miss the customer's actual need |
| **Systems Thinking** | The roadmap should reflect how the customer's systems, data, and people interact — not just what the software will do | Roadmap ignores data readiness or organizational change requirements; delivery stalls in Phase 3 on dependencies no one mapped |

**Gate question:** Would a team member joining this project mid-stream — or an agent acting on this roadmap — understand exactly what success looks like for each epic — and is the scope bounded enough to deliver it?

*CPS in focus at this gate:* Collaborative Communication, Judgment & Reasoning, Creative Direction & Taste

---

### Phase 2: Design
**Stage Gate: Architecture Sign-Off**

Agents begin doing real construction work: generating solution designs, architecture artifacts, and process maps. The consultant's role shifts from producer to director. This is the phase where the AI-Augmented trap is most visible — consultants who stay in producer mode lose the efficiency benefit and compromise their ability to validate what's been generated.

| Skill | Why it matters at this gate | What breaks when it's absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Intent Writing** | Every agent-assisted design artifact starts with a prompt or intent statement. Quality here determines whether output is usable or requires complete regeneration. | Consultant prompts agents vaguely; spends more time editing agent outputs than a human design would have taken; velocity benefits disappear |
| **Human-AI Orchestration** | The consultant must decide what agents design, what humans design, and how closely to supervise the blend across a range of task types | Consultant either delegates high-stakes design decisions without review, or over-supervises low-stakes tasks — neither produces the right outcome |
| **Output Validation** | Architecture sign-off is a formal gate. Validation must be structured and documented — not an impression formed on visual review. | Architecture is "approved" based on whether it looks complete; errors in logic or fit pass through and compound in Phase 3 |
| **Change Co-Creation** | Architecture decisions made without customer input require expensive change management later; co-design in Phase 2 is far cheaper than course correction in Phase 4 | Customer sees architecture for the first time at sign-off; no co-design sessions occurred; surprises accumulate toward go-live |

**Gate question:** Was the architecture reviewed against defined criteria — and did the customer actively participate in shaping it, or only approve it?

*CPS in focus at this gate:* Creative Direction & Taste, Judgment & Reasoning, Collaborative Communication

---

### Phase 3: Deliver
**Stage Gate: Approved Release Plan**

This is the highest-velocity phase in an AI-native model. Agents are building continuously. The consultant's primary job is not construction — it is orchestration, discernment, and continuous validation across a fast-moving, high-volume build cycle. This is the phase most likely to expose automation complacency.

| Skill | Why it matters at this gate | What breaks when it's absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Discernment** | Agents produce plausible-looking output at speed. The consultant must maintain calibrated vigilance — not just accept work that appears complete. | Subtle logic errors, scope misalignments, and data assumption gaps accumulate; they are cheap to fix at Phase 3 and expensive at Phase 4 |
| **Output Validation** | At delivery velocity, validation must be fast AND systematic. Ad hoc spot-checks are insufficient for a release plan gate. | Different team members apply different quality standards; error patterns are invisible until integration testing; the release plan reflects aspiration, not what was actually built |
| **Human-AI Orchestration** | Build tasks vary enormously in how safely they can be delegated. Calibrated judgment must be maintained across the full build cycle — not just at obvious risk points. | All tasks are treated as equally safe to delegate; the consultant loses situation awareness of what was built, by what logic, under what constraints; no one can explain the system to the customer |
| **Intent Writing** | New sub-tasks surface throughout delivery and each requires a new intent statement. Intent is not set once at kickoff — it must be iteratively refined. | The original intent statement is assumed to cover all emerging decisions; agents fill gaps with inference; output drifts from customer expectations incrementally and invisibly |
| **Responsible AI Sensing** | At delivery velocity, bias, privacy exposure, or compliance risk can be introduced by agent-generated outputs before anyone explicitly reviews for it. The consultant must maintain active awareness of these risks across the build cycle — not just at formal review points. | Agent-generated segmentation logic, recommendation outputs, or automated decision pathways introduce disparate impact or privacy exposure that no one flagged because it wasn't in the validation criteria |
| **Systems Thinking** | During a fast build, second-order effects become visible. The consultant must monitor for system drift before it compounds across interdependent agent-built components. | Tasks are managed in isolation; integration failures and downstream process impacts surface late; the release plan gate is reached with a system that works in parts but not as a whole |

**Gate question:** Does the release plan reflect what was actually built — was each component validated against the intent and outcomes defined in Phases 0 and 1 — and have responsible AI risks been actively reviewed, not just assumed absent?

*CPS in focus at this gate:* Judgment & Reasoning, Information Discernment, Adaptive Learning

---

### Phase 4: Deploy
**Stage Gate: UAT Sign-Off + Formal Go/No-Go**

Deployment is the point of irreversibility. The Go/No-Go decision is the highest-stakes judgment call in the lifecycle — and in an AI-native model, the consultant is frequently forming that judgment about work that was largely agent-generated. This gate is where discernment is most consequential and most likely to fail under time and stakeholder pressure.

| Skill | Why it matters at this gate | What breaks when it's absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Discernment** | The Go/No-Go requires a clear, defensible judgment about whether the system is ready — not whether it looks ready or whether stakeholders are ready to move on | Go/No-Go is decided on UAT completion rate rather than outcome-readiness; known issues are rationalized away under deployment pressure; post-go-live failures were detectable |
| **Output Validation** | UAT is a formal validation event. Its criteria must trace back to the success criteria defined in Phase 0 — otherwise it is testing the wrong things. | UAT validates features rather than outcomes; the Phase 0 success standard is never referenced; the system goes live and fails to deliver the value the engagement was designed to produce |
| **Change Co-Creation** | UAT with genuine customer participation produces higher-quality sign-off and reduces post-go-live adoption friction significantly | Customer UAT is a formality; real testing happened internally; customer-facing surprises begin immediately after go-live; adoption drops |
| **Human-AI Orchestration** | Post-deployment, some monitoring and optimization work transitions to agents. This handoff must be explicitly designed — roles, boundaries, and escalation paths defined before go-live. | No explicit plan exists for what agents manage vs. what humans manage post-go-live; ongoing monitoring falls through the cracks; system drift begins immediately |

**Gate question:** Can the consultant make a defensible, evidence-based case that this system is ready to deliver the outcomes defined in Phase 0 — and does the customer share that confidence?

*CPS in focus at this gate:* Judgment & Reasoning, Collaborative Communication, Information Discernment

---

### Phase 5: Adopt/Consume
**Stage Gate: Release Notes + Customer-Accepted Handoff Package**

In an AI-native model, Phase 5 is structurally different from traditional hypercare. Agents can continue optimizing in production. The system is not static. The handoff is not a clean break — it is a transfer of governance responsibility. A customer team that cannot make sound human-AI orchestration decisions after the consultant leaves is not ready for handoff, regardless of what the documentation says.

| Skill | Why it matters at this gate | What breaks when it's absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Outcome Framing** | Adoption is only meaningful measured against the outcomes defined in Phase 0. The handoff package must include a measurement plan, not just system documentation. | Handoff documents what was built; no plan exists for measuring whether it achieves the outcomes that justified the investment; success is never confirmed |
| **Change Co-Creation** | Customers who co-created the solution drive their own adoption. Phase 5 should reinforce that ownership — not transfer a finished product back to them. | Adoption is treated as a training problem; documentation and training are delivered; customer engagement drops; the system is used less than its capability warrants |
| **Systems Thinking** | In production, agentic systems introduce ongoing feedback loops that can drift without monitoring. Governance for ongoing system health must be designed into the handoff explicitly. | Handoff is a clean break; no feedback loops or monitoring design are included; system drift is discovered reactively, after customer-visible impact |
| **Human-AI Orchestration** | The customer's team now owns the orchestration decisions the consultant was making throughout delivery. That capability must be transferred deliberately — not assumed from tool training alone. | Customer team receives tool training but not orchestration judgment training; they cannot make sound delegate / supervise / override decisions independently; they return to manual processes or accept agent output without scrutiny |

**Gate question:** Does the customer have everything they need to measure outcomes, maintain the system, and govern their own human-AI orchestration decisions — without the consultant in the room?

*CPS in focus at this gate:* Collaborative Communication, Adaptive Learning, Systems Thinking

### MVG Orientation Brief

*For consultants onboarding to a QL + MVG pilot project.*

The Minimum Viable Governance framework is not a compliance layer. It is the structure that makes AI-native delivery repeatable rather than lucky. MVG gives the engagement its spine — a set of phase gates where humans make deliberate decisions before work advances. Without it, agentic delivery is fast but ungoverned. With it, speed and rigor coexist.

**The governing logic in one sentence:** Agents execute quickly, plausibly, and without self-doubt — the gates are where humans catch what agents cannot.

**What changes in an AI-native engagement:**
- Build compresses significantly — agents do construction work that previously took weeks
- Intent Definition becomes a first-class phase — what you ask agents to do matters more than how fast they do it
- Refinement and Validation become the new critical path — not a phase-end check, but a continuous loop
- The gates become more consequential, not less — because errors that reach a gate have been amplified by agent velocity

**What stays the same:**
- The customer still needs to own the outcome
- The consultant is still accountable for quality and fit
- The engagement still fails if the wrong thing is built fast

**Human-led, agent-powered** is the operating principle. Agents accelerate execution. Consultants lead strategy, validate output, and govern the decisions that cannot be automated.

The six MVG phases and their stage gates:

| Phase | Name | Stage Gate |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Internal Onboarding | Validated Success Criteria |
| 1 | Prepare | Customer-Approved Engagement Roadmap (Epics) |
| 2 | Design | Architecture Sign-Off |
| 3 | Deliver | Approved Release Plan |
| 4 | Deploy | UAT Sign-Off + Formal Go/No-Go |
| 5 | Adopt/Consume | Release Notes + Customer-Accepted Handoff Package |

Each gate is a deliberate decision point. Arriving at a gate without the skills to make a defensible decision at it is the primary risk this enablement addresses.

### Gate-by-Gate Flight Guide

Each gate entry covers the same ground: what to land with, the skills that are load-bearing at this stop, what a good gate decision looks like, and what to carry into the next phase. Landing and takeoff are one card — everything you need to navigate this gate is here.

#### Phase 0: Internal Onboarding
**Stage Gate: Validated Success Criteria**

**Landing — what to arrive with:**
Before this gate closes, the team needs a shared, measurable definition of what success looks like for this engagement — in outcome terms, not deliverable terms. The success criteria validated here are the quality standard every downstream gate will be measured against. If they're vague now, every gate that follows has no teeth.

**Skills load-bearing at this gate:**

| Skill | Why it's critical here |
|---|---|
| **Outcome Framing** | Success criteria must describe what changes for the customer — not what gets built. A checklist of deliverables is not a success standard. |
| **Intent Writing** | The engagement roadmap built in Phase 1 is grounded in intent established here. Vague intent at Phase 0 produces unpredictable agent behavior in Phase 2 and beyond. |
| **Data Quality Judgment** | Before agents are scoped for any task, the underlying data must be assessed as fit for agentic use — not just available. |

**Gate question:** Does the team have a shared, measurable definition of success — and has the customer's data been assessed as fit for the agentic workflows being planned?

**Takeoff — carry this into Phase 1:**
The success criteria you validated here are the anchor for every epic in the Phase 1 roadmap. Take them into the first customer session and make them visible. If a proposed epic can't be traced to one of these criteria, that's a scope question — not a delivery task.

### Data Collection Mechanisms

Five mechanisms run in parallel throughout the pilot. Together they provide real-time signal, gate-level reflection, post-project depth, pattern synthesis, and field observation — the full picture needed to design the three-track program that follows.

**1. Feedback Loop — Always On**
Async, low-friction, continuous. Consultants drop skill observations into `#ql-learnings-feedback` (already planned by GT) in the flow of work. Not structured — just a habit prompt pinned to the channel: *"What was hard? What did you wish you had?"* Owned by GT / Lauren Hunt.

**2. Gate Pulse Check — At Each Submission**
3–5 structured questions tied to each gate artifact submission. Piggybacks on the ~30 min/week PM tracking cadence GT is already designing — no new submission event. Questions vary by phase and target the load-bearing skills at that gate. Example at Phase 0: *"How confident were you in the success criteria you validated? What would have made that easier?"* Data feeds directly into findings reports. Owned by Victoria Gailey / Michele Lubeck.

**3. Exit Interview — At Project Close**
20-minute structured conversation with the PM and one senior consultant per project at Phase 5 handoff or close. Three questions: What was hardest? What would you have wanted earlier? What did you invent that should be captured? This is the richest source for the full program design — self-report under no time pressure, with the full engagement arc visible in hindsight. Owned by Stephanie Flasch / GT team.

**4. Cohort Pattern Digest — Weekly**
GT is already planning a weekly cohort digest for pilot participants. A simple "skill observation" field added to the digest format lets consultants narrate what they're noticing about their own capability gaps in context. Low lift to add; high signal for L&D. Owned by GT / Lauren Hunt.

**5. Player Coach / AI Coach Observations — Weekly**
For pilot projects with a designated Regional AI Coach or PACE team member, a simple weekly observation template: *What skill gap did you observe? What did you do about it?* Field-level behavioral data that self-report cannot produce. Feeds directly into gap analysis validation for the full program. Owned by PACE team / Kristen Lappin.

## AI-Ready Base Layer: Experience Design

### Design Principle

The Base Layer is not a course. It is a content system — a set of lightweight, always-available resources that give consultants the vocabulary and job aids they need to engage with AI-native delivery from day one. Every piece is designed to be pulled up in the flow of work, not completed in a learning event.

The Base Layer has one job per skill: **name the thing, establish the stakes, and give practitioners something they can use today.** Conceptual depth lives in the Proficiency Track. The Base Layer gets them far enough to know what they don't know yet.

**Delivery split:**
- **Slack canvases** — job aids and decision frameworks for skills that practitioners need at their fingertips during active work (Intent Writing, Output Validation, Outcome Framing, Human-AI Orchestration, Data Quality Judgment, Customer AI Readiness)
- **Trailhead modules** — conceptual foundation for skills that require more setup before the job aid makes sense (Discernment, Systems Thinking, Change Co-Creation)

Slack canvases are maintained by the L&D team and pinned to relevant PS Slack channels. Trailhead modules are searchable and linked from the canvases where relevant. Together they form a single findable system, not a scattered set of documents.

### Slack Canvas: Intent Writing Reference Card

**Home:** Pinned in #ps-ai-native-delivery and #agentforce-delivery (or equivalent)
**Length:** 1–2 pages
**Update cadence:** Reviewed quarterly as tooling evolves

**Content:**
- One-paragraph definition distinguishing a **prompt** (instruction to an AI tool) from an **intent statement** (structured specification for a delivery-context agent action)
- The five elements of a well-formed intent statement:
  1. **Goal** — what the agent should produce
  2. **Constraints** — what it must not do or include
  3. **Success criteria** — how you'll know the output is right
  4. **Edge cases** — situations the agent should handle explicitly
  5. **Guardrails** — escalation or stop conditions if something goes wrong
- Side-by-side example: weak prompt vs. strong intent statement for the same delivery scenario
- One-line diagnostic: *"If an agent executed this exactly as written and you were surprised by the output — that's an intent problem, not a tool problem."*
- Link to Proficiency Track intent writing module for deeper practice

**Design note for the assigned designer:** The worked example should use a Salesforce PS scenario — a discovery brief, a solution design request, or a UAT script generation task. Avoid generic chatbot examples. The goal is instant recognizability for a consultant on a live engagement.

### Slack Canvas: Output Validation Decision Protocol

**Home:** Pinned in #ps-ai-native-delivery and linked from Intent Writing canvas
**Length:** 1 page
**Update cadence:** Reviewed when new deliverable types are added to scope

**Content:**
- Four-step decision protocol for any AI-generated work product:
  1. **Accept** — output meets criteria; move downstream
  2. **Accept with edits** — output is directionally right; minor gaps corrected by consultant
  3. **Regenerate with revised intent** — output missed the mark; diagnose why before re-prompting (intent gap? data problem? wrong tool? misapplied criteria?)

- One sentence on continuous validation loops: *"Validation is not a phase-end gate. It is a recurring step at every handoff — between agent and consultant, between consultant and customer, between phase and phase."*

### Slack Canvas: Human-AI Orchestration Decision Framework

**Home:** Pinned in #ps-ai-native-delivery
**Length:** 1 page
**Update cadence:** Reviewed quarterly as agent capabilities evolve

**Content:**

### Trailhead Module: Discernment in AI-Native Delivery

**Format:** Single Trailhead unit (~20–30 minutes) within a broader AI-Native Delivery trail
**Searchable via:** "AI output review," "automation complacency," "Responsible AI consulting"

**Learning objectives:** After completing this unit, the learner will be able to (1) define automation complacency and explain why AI-native delivery increases its risk, (2) describe what Responsible AI Sensing requires of a consultant, and (3) identify at least three conditions that make sound discernment harder under delivery pressure.

**Content arc:**
1. **What discernment is** — evaluating AI-generated output and making sound, defensible decisions about it. Not exploration or possibility-sensing. Convergent judgment.
2. **The complacency problem** — when output looks complete and polished, scrutiny naturally decreases. This is not a personal failing; it is a documented cognitive pattern (automation bias) that worsens as tools become more reliable. Name it so practitioners can catch it in themselves.
3. **One vivid example from outside software** — aviation or healthcare: a domain where well-designed automation increased operator complacency with measurable consequences. The goal is to make the risk feel real and non-abstract before it lands in a PS context.
4. **What it looks like on a PS engagement** — a short scenario: a consultant at Phase 3 velocity receives an agent-generated solution design that looks complete. What are the conditions that make it hardest to scrutinize? (Time pressure, stakeholder momentum, visual polish, prior trust in the tool.)
5. **Responsible AI Sensing** — the sub-skill of noticing risks that weren't in the brief: bias, privacy exposure, compliance risk introduced by agent-generated outputs. Framed as a professional skill, not just an ethical policy.
6. **One practical takeaway** — link to the Output Validation canvas; a note that the Proficiency Track includes pressure scenarios specifically designed to practice discernment when it's hardest.

**Design note for the assigned designer:** The aviation/healthcare example is load-bearing — it needs to be specific enough to be credible, not a generic "pilots trusted autopilot too much" summary. The Air France 447 incident or a documented healthcare automation complacency study would work. Check FranklinCovey's Innovate for Impact program already in circulation — it uses "Discern" with a different meaning (opportunity-sensing). This module should make the distinction explicit so there's no confusion between the two.

### Trailhead Module: Systems Thinking for AI-Native Delivery

**Format:** Single Trailhead unit (~20–30 minutes) within the AI-Native Delivery trail
**Searchable via:** "systems thinking," "agentic delivery risk," "error propagation"

**Learning objectives:** After completing this unit, the learner will be able to (1) define four core systems thinking concepts in plain language, (2) explain how agentic delivery introduces failure modes that linear thinking misses, and (3) name at least two AI-specific system risks to map for at the start of an engagement.

**Content arc:**
1. **Four core concepts, plain language** — feedback loops, second-order effects, leverage points, and delays. One delivery-context example for each. No jargon. No engineering diagrams.
2. **Why it matters more in agentic delivery** — agents are active participants in the system with their own failure modes. Three AI-specific risks: error propagation (one flawed output moves downstream through multiple agents before a human sees it), context drift (agent optimizes for a proxy rather than actual intent over time), goal displacement (agent behavior diverges from stated intent as conditions change).
3. **A short mapping prompt** — *"Before your next engagement, identify: (1) the three highest-stakes agent-human handoffs, (2) what a failure at each handoff looks like, and (3) what second-order effects that failure triggers."* Not a formal methodology — a habit.
4. **One practical takeaway** — link to Proficiency Track systems thinking module where a full scenario mapping exercise is built. Note that the Immersive Cohort includes a systems mapping activity at the start of Week 1.

**Design note for the assigned designer:** This module should feel accessible to a consultant with no systems thinking background. Avoid academic framing. The Donella Meadows "Thinking in Systems" vocabulary is the right source material — her definitions are unusually practitioner-friendly. The PS-specific examples (error propagation, context drift, goal displacement) are the differentiating content; don't let the generic concepts crowd them out.

### Trailhead Module: Change Co-Creation in AI-Native Delivery

**Format:** Single Trailhead unit (~20–30 minutes) within the AI-Native Delivery trail
**Searchable via:** "co-creation," "customer collaboration," "AI-native change management"

**Learning objectives:** After completing this unit, the learner will be able to (1) distinguish co-creation from stakeholder engagement and explain why the difference matters in AI-native delivery, (2) describe what scope governance under acceleration requires, and (3) identify one co-design format they could use in a Phase 1 or Phase 2 working session.

**Content arc:**
1. **The distinction** — stakeholder engagement: present work and ask for feedback. Co-creation: design together in real time. The difference is not a collaboration preference; in AI-native delivery it is structural. When AI compresses timelines, customers who weren't present for the shaping of work will not own its outcomes.
2. **Why traditional change management breaks** — traditional change management is sequenced after design. When AI can generate working artifacts in hours, there is no "after design." Customers need to be in the room while the shaping happens.
3. **Scope governance under acceleration** — AI creates momentum. Customers get excited. Scope expands faster and less visibly than in traditional delivery. This section specifically addresses how to distinguish input that should reshape intent from input that represents expansion — and how to hold that line without killing the collaborative energy co-creation depends on.
4. **One concrete co-design format** — a 60-minute working session structure a consultant could run at Phase 1 or Phase 2: framing the problem, generating options with AI live in the session, capturing customer reactions as intent refinement rather than scope addition, closing with a documented decision.
5. **One practical takeaway** — link to Proficiency Track change co-creation module for facilitation practice; note that the Immersive Cohort includes a live co-design session in the mock project.

**Design note for the assigned designer:** The scope governance piece (point 3) is the most original content here — it's not in any standard change management curriculum. Make it specific: give consultants a concrete example of what "scope expansion disguised as input" sounds like in a customer session, and what a skilled response looks like.

| Change Co-Creation | Trailhead unit | AI-Native Delivery trail | ~25 min |

**What the Base Layer does not do:** It does not develop judgment under pressure. It does not give consultants practice with realistic failure scenarios. It does not build the habits that sustained AI-native delivery requires. Those are Proficiency Track and Immersive Cohort jobs. The Base Layer gets consultants to a shared vocabulary and a set of job aids they can use immediately — no more, no less.

## Proficiency Track: Experience Design

### Design Principle

The proficiency track is not a module library. It is a single, sequenced experience built around one delivery scenario — a fictional PS engagement that runs from pre-Phase 0 through handoff. Every skill is introduced at the moment it would be needed in a real engagement, practiced inside that context, and connected to what came before and what comes next.

The scenario itself is interchangeable — it can be a purpose-built fictional engagement or a lite version of the Quantum Leap mock project used in the Immersive Cohort. What matters is that it is one consistent context, not a rotating set of examples. Learners should feel like they are running an engagement, not completing a course.

If the Quantum Leap mock project scenario is used, the proficiency track operates as a **partial reveal** — learners see the customer context and Phase 0–1 setup but not the complications that emerge in Phases 2–3. The cohort is where the full scenario unfolds. This creates continuity across the two experiences and gives learners a reason to attend the cohort beyond credential completion.

---

### The Delivery Playbook

The primary artifact learners build throughout the track. A structured running document — maintained in a Slack canvas or Google Doc — where learners record decisions, document assumptions, capture job aids they've applied, and note what they'd do differently. It is not assessed. It is theirs to keep and bring into the Immersive Cohort or their next live project.

The Playbook has one entry per skill cluster, added progressively as learners move through the track. By the close it functions as a personal reference for AI-native delivery — grounded in their own thinking, not a generic template.

Player Coaches on live projects can use a learner's Playbook as a coaching anchor: what did you set up in the proficiency track, and how did the real engagement compare?

---

### Experience Arc

---

#### Opening: Orientation (~1 hour)

**Purpose:** Surface what learners already believe before the learning begins. Set the stakes.

Learners start with a **diagnostic scenario set** — 6 short situations drawn from real AI-native delivery failure modes. No scoring. Learners record their instinct for each: what would they do, and why? These responses are saved and returned to at the close.

This is followed by the **scenario brief** — a short narrative introduction to the engagement: who the customer is, what they've asked for, what the delivery team looks like, and what's already at risk before Phase 0 opens. Learners know from the start that the engagement has complications.

*Modalities:* Written diagnostic → narrative scenario brief (short video or written case)

---

#### Act 1: Before You Build — Phases 0 and 1 (~7 hours)

**Purpose:** Build the skills that determine whether the engagement is set up to succeed. Mistakes here are cheap to fix now — and expensive later.

*CPS developed in this act:* Judgment & Reasoning, Systems Thinking, Collaborative Communication, Information Discernment — the foundational capabilities that make it possible to set up an engagement that won't fall apart under delivery pressure.

Skills covered in sequence:
1. Customer AI Readiness Assessment
2. Data Quality Judgment (Salesforce-specific: Data Cloud readiness for agentic workflows)
3. Assumption Surfacing and Documentation
4. Outcome Framing
5. Intent Writing (including prompt template design for reuse)

**Module format — consistent across all skills in this act:**

- **Concept input** (10–15 min): What is this skill, why does it matter at this exact point in the engagement, and what does it look like in a Salesforce PS context. Short article or video — no longer than needed.
- **Scenario decision** (20–30 min): A moment in the engagement where the learner makes a judgment call. Two or three plausible choices are presented. Learners choose and see a realistic consequence — not a grade, a narrative outcome. Some consequences are immediate; some are flagged as "you'll see the impact of this in Act 2."
- **Playbook contribution** (5–10 min): Learners add a structured entry to their Delivery Playbook for this skill — a decision, an assumption documented, a job aid applied.

**Act 1 gate check:**
Before moving to Act 2, learners review their Playbook entries and answer: *Given what I've documented so far, would I pass Phase 0 on this engagement? What's still underdefined?* This is a reflection, not a gate. Learners proceed regardless — but the question primes them to notice the gaps Act 2 will expose.

*Modalities:* Concept input → scenario decision with consequence → Playbook entry → gate reflection

---

#### Act 2: While You're Delivering — Phases 2 and 3 (~8 hours)

**Purpose:** Build the skills that operate at delivery velocity. This act deliberately introduces time pressure and volume — the conditions under which automation complacency, validation shortcuts, and orchestration failures actually happen.

*CPS developed in this act:* Judgment & Reasoning, Information Discernment, Adaptive Learning, Creative Direction & Taste — the capabilities that keep quality and judgment intact when speed and volume are working against them.

Skills covered in sequence:
1. Agentforce Agent Design Principles
2. Failure Mode Literacy (Agentforce, Claude, Cursor — tool-specific)
3. Human-AI Orchestration
4. Discernment
5. Output Validation
6. Responsible AI Sensing
7. Einstein Trust Layer and Data Governance

**Module format — adjusted for pressure:**

The concept input shortens in Act 2. Learners are assumed to be in flow. Scenario decisions are more frequent and faster — some present a time constraint explicitly ("the customer is in 30 minutes"). Consequences from Act 1 decisions surface here without warning, connecting earlier choices to current pressure.

At least one scenario presents an agent output that is **plausible but wrong** — well-formatted, confident, and incorrect in a way that requires genuine scrutiny to catch. Learners who moved quickly through Act 1's assumption surfacing will find this harder. That's the point.

**Mid-point peer exchange:**
After the Discernment module, a short structured exchange — async or scheduled depending on cohort logistics. Learners share one decision from Act 2 they're confident about and one they're uncertain about. This is the first social moment in the track. It seeds the peer relationships the Immersive Cohort will build on and normalizes uncertainty as a professional stance rather than a gap.

*Modalities:* Short concept input → pressure scenario with consequence → peer exchange at midpoint → Playbook entry

---

#### Act 3: With the Customer and Beyond — Phases 4, 5, and Reuse (~6 hours)

**Purpose:** Build the skills that operate at the customer interface and create value beyond the engagement. The consequences of earlier decisions become visible here.

*CPS developed in this act:* Collaborative Communication, Creative Direction & Taste, Adaptive Learning, Systems Thinking — the capabilities that sustain trust with the customer, transfer capability rather than just deliverables, and ensure the learning from this engagement compounds into the next one.

Skills covered in sequence:
1. Change Co-Creation and Scope Governance Under Acceleration
2. Communicating AI-Generated Work
3. Consumption Model Literacy
4. HOK-to-Agentic Transition Judgment
5. Field Pattern Documentation

**Module format — customer voice introduced:**

Act 3 introduces written **customer stakeholder reactions** to decisions the learner made in Acts 1 and 2. An assumption that wasn't documented in Phase 0 surfaces as a scope dispute in Phase 4. A co-design session that was skipped in Phase 2 shows up as a resistant customer in UAT. These are not punishments — they are the natural consequence logic of the engagement playing out. Learners see that the track was coherent all along.

The **Field Pattern Documentation** module closes Act 3. Learners take two things from their Playbook — one intent statement and one validation decision — and write them up as reusable patterns for the next team. This is the contribution back to the golden patterns flywheel and makes the learning feel purposeful rather than self-contained.

*Modalities:* Concept input → customer-voice scenario → Playbook entry → pattern contribution

---

#### Close: The Debrief (~1 hour)

**Purpose:** Make the learning visible. Set up what comes next.

**Return to the opening diagnostic:** Learners revisit the same 6 scenarios from the orientation and record their responses again. No comparison is scored — learners compare their own before and after. The shift in their thinking is the evidence of learning and it's self-generated.

**Playbook review and forward brief:** Learners review their completed Playbook and write one paragraph: *Given what I know now, what am I most uncertain or underprepared for going into a real AI-native engagement?* This becomes their personal brief — for the Immersive Cohort if nominated, or for their next live project if not.

If the Quantum Leap mock project scenario was used, the close explicitly frames the scenario as unresolved. The cohort is where it continues.

*Modalities:* Reflective diagnostic → personal synthesis → forward brief

## Discovery Workstreams for Junior Researchers

The following workstreams are designed for junior team members to pick up independently. Each one advances a specific skill cluster from hypothesis to evidence. The goal is not to produce a final recommendation — it is to gather enough grounded material that the L&D team can make informed decisions about what to build, and how.

Each workstream has three parts: a research question, a set of discovery tasks, and a description of what a useful output looks like.

---

### How to Approach This Work

Before starting, read the skill definition for your assigned cluster in the framework above. Your job is to answer the research question with evidence — examples, models, existing programs, or documented approaches — not to generate opinions or generalize from one example. When you find something useful, note the source, what it says, and why it's relevant.

If you're not sure whether a source is worth citing, ask: *"Does this help us understand what good looks like for this skill in a professional services context?"* If yes, include it. If it's general or aspirational, set it aside.

---

### Workstream 1: Intent Writing

**Research question:** What does a well-structured intent statement look like in practice, and what teaching approaches help people learn to write them?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Find 2–3 examples of intent statement templates or frameworks used in AI-assisted workflows, software development, or consulting contexts. What elements do they include? (Goal, constraints, success criteria, edge cases?)
- Research how user stories are taught in Agile environments — what makes a good user story, and where do beginners typically go wrong?
- Look for any existing Salesforce internal guidance on prompting or intent writing — check Slack canvases, Trailhead, or internal wikis
- Find one example of a training exercise or practice activity used to teach requirements writing or prompt specification

**What a useful output looks like:** A one-page summary of 2–3 intent writing models or templates, with notes on what each includes and a recommendation for which elements are most transferable to our delivery context. Include at least one example of a teaching exercise.

---

### Workstream 2: Discernment

**Research question:** What does it look like when someone exercises good discernment over AI-generated output — and how do you teach it?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Research "automation complacency" or "automation bias" — what is it, when does it happen, and what conditions make it worse or better? Look for examples from aviation, healthcare, or software review contexts
- Find examples of training approaches that specifically practice skeptical review of plausible-but-wrong outputs. (Medical diagnosis training and financial audit training both use this approach — look at how they structure exercises)
- Look for any existing guidance inside Salesforce on reviewing or quality-checking AI outputs
- Interview or survey one or two consultants who are actively using AI tools in delivery: ask them how they decide whether to trust an agent's output, and what they look for when something seems off

**What a useful output looks like:** A summary of what automation complacency is, with one real-world example from a non-software domain. A description of one training approach that practices deliberate skepticism. Notes from any consultant conversations, anonymized. A draft list of 4–6 "red flags" a consultant should look for when reviewing AI-generated work.

---

### Workstream 3: Output Validation

**Research question:** What does a structured, repeatable validation process look like for AI-generated work products — and what makes it different from traditional quality review?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Look at how audit, journalism, or academic peer review approaches structured review of work you didn't produce. What criteria or checklists do they use?
- Find examples of validation rubrics — grids that break down quality into specific, assessable dimensions rather than a single pass/fail judgment. Education and software testing both use these.
- Research what types of errors AI tools most commonly produce in the kinds of outputs consultants generate (documents, process designs, solution architectures). Look for any Salesforce field data, or external practitioner reports.
- Draft a simple validation checklist for one common PS deliverable (e.g., a discovery document or a solution design) — what would "done well" look like, dimension by dimension?

**What a useful output looks like:** A comparison of 2–3 structured review approaches from different fields, with notes on what transfers to consulting. A draft validation rubric for one PS deliverable type. A short list of the most common AI output failure modes relevant to our work.

---

### Workstream 4: Outcome Framing

**Research question:** What does outcomes-based consulting look like in practice, and what are the most common ways consultants slip back into deliverable-focused thinking?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Research OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) — not the theory, but how they're used in practice. What's the most common mistake people make when writing OKRs, and why does it matter?
- Look for examples of how consulting engagements have defined and measured outcomes rather than deliverables. Any case studies from professional services, public sector, or technology implementation contexts are useful.
- Talk to one or two delivery leads or project managers: ask them how they define success with a customer at the start of an engagement, and whether that definition holds through delivery or tends to shift
- Find one facilitation method or workshop technique used to help customers articulate what success looks like in measurable terms

**What a useful output looks like:** A short summary of the deliverable-vs-outcome distinction with one concrete example from a real engagement or case study. Notes from delivery lead conversations. A description of one facilitation technique for surfacing outcome-based success criteria. A list of 3–4 warning signs that a project has lost its outcome anchor.

---

### Workstream 5: Human-AI Orchestration

**Research question:** How do practitioners decide what to delegate to AI agents versus do themselves — and what training or guidance exists to help them make better decisions?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Research how aviation or healthcare train practitioners to work alongside automation — specifically, how they teach function allocation (what humans do vs. what automation does) and when to override or take over
- Look for any decision frameworks or heuristics that help practitioners decide whether to delegate a task to an AI tool. Even informal blog posts or practitioner guides from software development communities are relevant here.
- Survey or interview 2–3 consultants actively using AI in delivery: what's their personal rule of thumb for when they trust an agent versus when they stay hands-on? Do they have criteria, or is it intuition?
- Look for any Salesforce internal guidance on AI tool use in delivery — Slack channels, playbooks, or onboarding materials

**What a useful output looks like:** A summary of one function allocation model from a non-software domain, with notes on what transfers. A synthesis of practitioner interviews: what criteria do people actually use? A draft decision framework with 4–6 questions a consultant could ask before delegating a task to an agent.

---

### Workstream 6: Systems Thinking

**Research question:** What does systems thinking look like when applied to an AI-native delivery engagement — and are there teaching approaches that make it accessible to consultants without a formal systems background?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Find an accessible, practitioner-friendly introduction to systems thinking concepts — feedback loops, second-order effects, leverage points. Look for applications in organizational or business contexts, not just engineering.
- Research "sociotechnical systems" — the idea that technical and human/organizational systems must be designed together, not separately. Find one real-world example where ignoring this caused a project failure.
- Look for examples of how consultants or project managers use system mapping — process maps, dependency maps, risk registers — and consider how these tools could be adapted to surface AI-specific risks (error propagation, context drift, goal displacement)
- Talk to a delivery lead who has worked on a complex, multi-system engagement: ask them what surprised them about how things connected, and what they would have mapped differently at the start

**What a useful output looks like:** A plain-language explanation of 3–4 core systems thinking concepts, with one example each from a delivery context. A description of one system-mapping exercise adapted for AI-native engagements. Notes from a delivery lead conversation. A draft list of AI-specific system risks consultants should map for at the start of an engagement.

---

### Workstream 7: Change Co-Creation

**Research question:** What does real-time co-creation with customers look like in a delivery context — and how is it different from traditional stakeholder engagement or change management?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Research participatory design or co-design methods — what are the core principles, and what makes them different from presenting work and asking for feedback?
- Find 2–3 facilitation techniques used in co-design sessions. Look for methods that are practical in a working-session format (not multi-day design sprints). The Luma Institute, IDEO, and d.school all have accessible practitioner resources.
- Look for examples of how customer co-creation has been used in technology implementation or consulting contexts — cases where involving customers earlier changed the outcome
- Interview one PS consultant who has run a working session with a customer: what was the format, how did they capture input, and how did it affect the project direction?

**What a useful output looks like:** A summary of co-design principles, contrasted with traditional stakeholder engagement. Descriptions of 2–3 facilitation techniques with enough detail to use them. Notes from the consultant interview. A draft session outline for a 60-minute customer co-design session at Phase 1 or Phase 2 of an MVG engagement.

---

### Workstream 8: Data Quality Judgment

**Research question:** How do practitioners assess whether a customer's data is fit for agentic use — and what frameworks or diagnostics exist to guide that assessment?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Research how data quality is defined and assessed in enterprise AI and data engineering contexts. Look for frameworks that break it into specific dimensions — completeness, accuracy, consistency, timeliness, fitness for purpose. What does each dimension mean in practice?
- Find examples of pre-engagement data readiness assessments used by technology consulting firms or AI implementation teams. What do they look at, and what does a "not ready" finding look like?
- Look for case studies or post-mortems where AI deployments failed because of data quality problems that weren't caught before delivery began. What was missed and why?
- Talk to one or two Salesforce PS delivery leads or technical architects: how do they currently assess data readiness before scoping an engagement, and what surprises them most often in production?

**What a useful output looks like:** A short definition of data quality with 4–6 specific dimensions relevant to agentic delivery. A description of one pre-engagement data assessment approach with enough detail to adapt it. Notes from practitioner conversations. A draft checklist of data quality questions a consultant should answer before committing to an AI-native delivery model with a customer.

---

### Workstream 9: Customer AI Readiness Assessment

**Research question:** What does a rigorous pre-engagement readiness assessment look like for AI-native delivery — and how do practitioners have honest conversations with customers whose readiness is incomplete?

**Discovery tasks:**
- Research how enterprise AI readiness is assessed in practice. Look for frameworks from consulting firms, technology vendors, or academic sources that define what "ready" means across organizational, technical, and cultural dimensions.
- Find examples of readiness assessment tools or questionnaires used in technology implementation contexts — not just AI. What dimensions do they cover? How are results communicated to customers?
- Look for any internal Salesforce guidance on customer readiness for Agentforce or agentic delivery — check Slack, internal wikis, or playbooks
- Interview one or two PS leaders or Strategic Builders: how do they currently assess whether a customer is ready for AI-native delivery? What are the clearest signs a customer is not ready — and how do they handle that conversation?

**What a useful output looks like:** A summary of 3–5 dimensions of AI readiness (organizational, data, technical, cultural, sponsorship) with a plain-language description of each. Notes from practitioner interviews, anonymized. A draft conversation guide for the readiness discussion — how to raise concerns without losing the customer relationship. A simple scoring or traffic-light model a consultant could use to summarize a customer's readiness across dimensions.

### Entry 1: Tri-Track Simultaneous Build & Audit

**Source:** Field proposal shared via Slack, June 2026. Full document: *Quantum Leap Methodology Extension: Tri-Track Simultaneous Build & Audit.*
**Phase context:** Build Phase — extends the standard AI-Native Delivery Build Day Loop.

#### Summary

Once an Intent Architect (IA) confirms an Intent Statement is ready-to-build, the Agent Orchestrator (AO) dispatches three specialized agents in parallel isolation:

- **Track 1 (QA):** MeshMesh QA Agent generates UAT test cases and traceability matrices from the IS acceptance criteria
- **Track 2 (Code Quality):** MeshMesh Developer Agent generates Apex unit tests targeting 90% minimum class-level coverage
- **Track 3 (Execution):** Build Agents (MeshMesh for declarative config; Claude Code for Apex, MuleSoft, automation) write the actual code, configuration, and flows

The three tracks converge in a **Convergence Audit** before any artifact reaches the Daily Demo:
1. **Pipeline Gate** — Track 3 build must physically pass Track 2 unit tests. Bypasses strictly disallowed.
2. **Review Agent Cross-Check** — A separate Review Agent cross-references Track 1 QA scenarios against Track 3 build logic to surface unhandled edge cases.
3. **HITL Gap Analysis** — Logic & Integration Engineer (LIE) and Logic Validator (LV) review automated findings and issue one of three responses: **Approve**, **Refine** (targeted micro-iteration on a specific workstream), or **Reject and Redirect** (Agent Grounding updated, build track restarted from scratch).

**The core insight:** Running build and testing as independent tracks prevents Build Agents from writing tests that artificially pass their own flawed logic. HITL reviewers are working from pre-triangulated artifacts — a fundamentally cleaner baseline than reviewing build output alone.

---

#### Role Definitions (Source Material for RACI Module)

| Role | Abbreviation | Responsibility |
|---|---|---|
| Intent Architect | IA | Confirms the Intent Statement is precise, constrained, and ready-to-build before dispatch |
| Agent Orchestrator | AO | Dispatches agents across tracks; updates Agent Grounding on Reject; manages dispatch latency |
| Logic & Integration Engineer | LIE | HITL on Track 2 (unit tests) and Track 3 (build); reviews convergence audit findings |
| Logic Validator | LV | HITL on Track 1 (QA/UAT test cases); confirms traceability matrices align with acceptance criteria |

These four roles are the primary source material for the **Project Roles and RACI in AI-Native Delivery** module identified in the program build list. They represent a more granular, delivery-tested role structure than the framework currently contains.

---

#### Mapping to Framework Skill Clusters

**Agentic Drift (new term — adopt across framework)**
The proposal names two specific methodology risks: *Agentic Drift* and *Rubber-Stamp Approvals.* Agentic Drift — where a Build Agent's output diverges from the Intent Statement constraints without anyone noticing — is a more practitioner-friendly and delivery-specific term than the framework's current "context drift" and "goal displacement." Recommend adopting Agentic Drift as the primary term in the Discernment and Systems Thinking sections, with context drift/goal displacement retained as definitional support.

**Discernment**
Rubber-Stamp Approvals is this proposal's name for automation complacency at the HITL review step. The Convergence Audit is specifically designed to prevent it by giving reviewers pre-triangulated artifacts rather than raw agent output. This is a strong concrete example for the Discernment Trailhead module — "what does the delivery environment look like when it's designed to support good discernment?" The Approve / Refine / Reject and Redirect response protocol maps directly to the Output Validation decision protocol (Accept / Accept with edits / Regenerate with revised intent / Escalate).

**Output Validation**
The core argument of the Tri-Track model — that you cannot reliably validate your own agent's output — is the most concrete available illustration of why independent validation matters. Specifically: a Build Agent writing its own unit tests will optimize for passing, not for coverage of the actual logic. This should be a named example in the Output Validation Proficiency Track module.

**Human-AI Orchestration**
The AO role as described here is the most operationally detailed picture of Human-AI Orchestration the framework currently has access to. Dispatch decisions, grounding management, latency tradeoffs, and the Reject-and-Redirect loop (update grounding, restart track) are all orchestration judgment calls. The four-question decision framework in the Base Layer canvas should be tested against this model.

**Systems Thinking**
The Convergence Audit is a practical example of designing a delivery system to catch error propagation before it reaches a customer-facing stage. The three-track architecture with a convergence node is a systems design, not just a process step. Useful as a teaching illustration for why system design matters — the Tri-Track model makes invisible drift visible by forcing independent outputs to converge.

**Intent Writing**
The proposal reinforces that the Intent Statement is load-bearing for all three tracks simultaneously — it is the "atomic baseline" from which QA, testing, and build all operate independently. A vague IS does not just produce a bad build; it produces three independently bad outputs that all converge on the same flawed premise. This strengthens the "errors in intent propagate through every downstream agent action" argument in Skill 1.

---

#### Open Questions for Program Design

- The proposal requests AO input on **dispatch latency** — this is a methodology question, but it has a skills implication: how does an AO decide when parallel dispatch is appropriate vs. when sequential execution reduces risk? This is an orchestration judgment call not yet covered in the framework.
- The 90% coverage gate (vs. the 85% referenced in the Slack summary) is a specific quality standard. The Output Validation rubric module should address how HITL reviewers evaluate whether a coverage gate is meaningful or just met.
- The **Agent Grounding** update step on Reject is referenced but not defined. What does updating grounding require of the AO as a skill? This may be a sub-skill of Intent Writing (diagnosing why an IS produced drift and how to constrain it more precisely) or of Human-AI Orchestration (knowing when a Reject warrants a grounding update vs. a simple re-prompt).

### Entry 2: Model Optimization Literacy ("Maxxxing")

**Source:** Slack message to working team, June 2026. Re: Claude performance issues flagged by pilot teams.
**Escalation path:** #llm-gateway-express-support — tag Rohith Reddy Gangasani / Sanjay Kumar / Sohail Cheppali Shaik (PS Claude lead).

#### Summary

Pilot teams are experiencing Claude latency issues (avg 2.5–3.5s time to first token, with spikes significantly beyond that). The root cause is not purely infrastructure — teams are not making informed choices about model selection and thinking level for the task at hand. From the field message: *"I don't think a lot of people understand the moves between models as far as pricing and performance, and the impact when they do change — that it can affect things they already have in progress."*

Key observations from pilot data:
- Lower models / less thinking = significantly faster responses
- Latency is not consistent across models
- Model switches mid-engagement can affect outputs and continuity in ways consultants don't anticipate

**The skill gap:** Consultants are treating model selection as a default setting, not a decision. Matching model capability and thinking level to task type — and understanding the trade-offs — is a practitioner judgment call, not a tool configuration step.

---

#### Mapping to Framework Skill Clusters

**Human-AI Orchestration (primary)**
Model selection is an orchestration decision. The four-question framework (Delegate or do? Supervise closely or trust and verify? Override or regenerate? Escalate or close?) implicitly assumes the consultant is dispatching the right tool for the task. Model optimization adds a prior question: *Is this model and thinking level appropriate for this task's complexity, latency tolerance, and cost profile?* This should be added to the Base Layer canvas as a fifth consideration and addressed explicitly in the Proficiency Track orchestration module.

**Adaptive Learning**
Model capabilities and pricing change faster than any curriculum. Knowing *today's* right model for a given task type is less important than knowing how to evaluate that question as the landscape shifts. This is a concrete instance of the adaptive learning pattern: consultants must be able to recalibrate their model choices between formal learning events, not wait for updated guidance.

**Intent Writing (adjacent)**
Thinking level is a parameter of how an agent processes an intent statement — heavier thinking = more thorough reasoning but slower response. Consultants writing intent statements for time-sensitive tasks (live customer sessions, daily demo prep) need to understand that model + thinking level is part of the dispatch decision, not separate from it.

---

#### Proposed Addition: Model Optimization as a Named Sub-Skill

**Candidate home:** Sub-skill of Human-AI Orchestration, parallel to the existing Responsible AI Sensing sub-skill under Discernment.

**Working definition:** The ability to select the appropriate model and thinking level for a given task — balancing capability, response latency, cost, and continuity — and to recalibrate that selection as models evolve and engagement conditions change.

**What it includes:**
- Understanding the capability / latency / cost trade-off across available models
- Recognizing which task types warrant higher thinking levels vs. faster, lighter responses
- Understanding how model switches mid-engagement can affect output continuity and agent grounding
- Knowing the escalation path when latency issues are infrastructure-related vs. configuration-related (#llm-gateway-express-support)

**What makes it harder in an AI-native context:** In a single-tool workflow, there is one model and one setting. In an agentic delivery environment, multiple agents may be running simultaneously at different thinking levels — the aggregate latency and cost impact of those choices compounds in ways that aren't visible from any single dispatch decision.

#### Open Questions for Program Design

- Does model optimization belong in the Base Layer (reference card), the Proficiency Track (decision module), or both? Given that pilot teams are hitting this now, a Base Layer reference card may be the most urgent deliverable.
- The escalation path (tagging specific individuals in #llm-gateway-express-support) is current as of June 2026 — this information will decay. The Base Layer material should teach the *pattern* of escalation (infrastructure issue vs. configuration issue) rather than hardcoding names and channels.
- Model pricing and capability information changes frequently. Any module covering this should be designed for easy update rather than embedded in a fixed course format.
